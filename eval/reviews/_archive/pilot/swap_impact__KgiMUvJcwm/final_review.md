## Summary  
The paper introduces **IgSeek**, a novel framework for antibody design that addresses the challenge of hallucinations in sequence inference for Complementarity-Determining Regions (CDRs). Instead of generating sequences from scratch, IgSeek retrieves structurally similar CDRs from a database using a **multi-channel equivariant graph neural network (MEGNN)** to encode backbone geometries. The retrieved sequences are then used to infer the target CDR sequence via ensemble and Bernoulli sampling. Experiments show that IgSeek outperforms state-of-the-art methods in **sequence recovery accuracy (AAR)** and **inference speed**, particularly for hyper-variable CDR-H3 loops. The approach is motivated by the observation that CDRs exhibit conserved structural motifs despite sequence diversity, and it leverages this to improve reliability and efficiency in antibody design.

## Strengths  
- **Novelty of the Retrieval-Based Approach**: IgSeek introduces a fundamentally different strategy for CDR sequence design by using **structure retrieval** instead of generative models. This is a significant departure from existing methods like ProteinMPNN or ESM-IF1, which are prone to hallucinations. The retrieval-based method aligns with recent trends in reducing hallucinations in protein design (e.g., retrieval-augmented prediction in AlphaFold2) and is well-motivated by the empirical observation of canonical CDR structures.  
- **Strong Performance on Sequence Recovery**: The experiments demonstrate that IgSeek achieves **higher AAR** than existing models on both **SAbDab-2024** and **STCRDab** datasets. Notably, it outperforms AntiFold and AbMPNN by at least **2.9% on light chain CDRs** and matches their performance on heavy chains. The **IgSeek⁺Kabsch** variant further improves results, showing the value of integrating RMSD as a secondary metric.  
- **Efficiency and Scalability**: IgSeek is **2.6x faster** than FoldSeek in structure retrieval and **20x faster** than generative models in sequence inference. This efficiency is critical for high-throughput antibody design and could significantly reduce computational bottlenecks in drug discovery pipelines.  
- **Equivariant Modeling**: The use of **E(3) equivariant neural networks** ensures that the model is invariant to rigid transformations of the input structure, which is a well-justified inductive bias for 3D structure modeling. The theoretical analysis in Section 3 and the visualization in Section 5.4 support the model's ability to capture meaningful structural relationships.  
- **Generalization to TCRs**: The model is tested on **T-Cell Receptor (TCR) CDRs**, showing that it generalizes beyond antibodies. This is a promising result, as it suggests the method could be applied to other **hyper-variable protein domains**.  

## Weaknesses  
- **Lack of Structural and Functional Validation**: While the paper reports **sequence recovery accuracy (AAR)**, it does not validate whether the predicted sequences **fold into the desired structures** or **bind to the intended antigens**. This is a critical gap, as the ultimate goal of antibody design is to produce **functionally viable sequences**. Without structural validation (e.g., using AlphaFold or Rosetta), it is unclear whether the method truly reduces hallucinations or merely aligns sequences to structurally similar CDRs.  
- **Limited Ablation Studies**: The paper includes only a **minimal ablation study** (e.g., IgSeek⁺Kabsch), but does not test the impact of key design choices such as the **multi-channel architecture**, **perturbation strategy**, or **READOUT function**. A more thorough ablation would strengthen the paper's contribution and provide deeper insights into the model's behavior.  
- **No Cross-Chain or Cross-CDR Evaluation**: The experiments do not assess whether the model generalizes across **different CDR types** (e.g., CDR-L1 vs. CDR-H3) or **different antibody chains** (e.g., heavy vs. light) without retraining. This is important for real-world applications where the model may be used for a variety of CDRs.  
- **Ambiguity in Key Definitions**: The paper does not clearly define **"isomorphic structure retrieval"** or **"structurally conserved sequence motifs"**, which are central to the methodology. This lack of clarity makes it difficult to assess the **novelty and theoretical underpinnings** of the approach.  
- **No Code or Database Availability**: The paper does not mention whether the **code or CDR vector database** is publicly available. This omission hinders reproducibility and limits the ability of the community to build upon the work.  
- **Missing Statistical Rigor**: The results are reported without **p-values or confidence intervals**, making it hard to assess whether the improvements over baselines are **statistically significant**. Additionally, the paper does not test the model on **noisy or partial structures**, which are common in real-world applications.  

## Questions  
- Does IgSeek's predicted sequence fold into the desired CDR structure? If not, how does the method address the issue of hallucinations in a way that generative models like ProteinMPNN or ESM-IF1 do not?  
- How does the performance of IgSeek change when the input CDR structure is **noisy or incomplete**? Is the method robust to such real-world scenarios?  
- What is the **exact architecture** of the MEGNN encoder (e.g., number of layers, hidden dimensions, activation functions)? How does the model's performance change with different architectures?  
- How does the **READOUT function** (e.g., mean pooling) affect the final sequence prediction? Are there alternative aggregation strategies that could improve performance?  
- What is the **value of K** used in the K-NN search, and how does the model's performance vary with different K values?  
- Is the **CDR vector database** publicly available, and if not, what are the plans for sharing it with the community?
