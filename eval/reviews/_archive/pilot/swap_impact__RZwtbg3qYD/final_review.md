## Summary
The paper introduces HOPE, a novel parameterization scheme for linear time-invariant (LTI) systems in state-space models (SSMs), based on the Markov parameters of a discrete Hankel operator. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that HOPE-SSM is more robust to initialization and training compared to existing models like S4 and S4D. They also demonstrate that HOPE-SSM has non-decaying memory within a fixed time window, which is beneficial for tasks with long-range dependencies. Empirical results on the sCIFAR-10 and Long-Range Arena (LRA) datasets support the claims, showing improved performance over several SSM baselines.

## Strengths
The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by connecting the expressiveness and numerical stability of SSMs to the properties of Hankel singular values. This provides a unified perspective on the challenges of initializing and training SSMs, which is a novel and insightful approach. The proposed HOPE parameterization is well-motivated and offers a clear advantage in terms of parameter efficiency and numerical stability. The empirical validation is thorough, with comparisons to multiple SSM baselines on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The results on the noise-padded sCIFAR-10 task are particularly compelling, as they demonstrate the long-term memory capabilities of HOPE-SSM. The paper also provides a detailed analysis of the perturbation sensitivity of S4D models, which supports the need for a more stable parameterization.

## Weaknesses
While the paper presents a novel parameterization, the experimental section lacks sufficient detail to fully assess the practical implications of the method. The training procedure, including optimizer, learning rate, and batch size, is not clearly described, making it difficult to reproduce the results or understand the extent to which the performance gains are due to the parameterization versus the training setup. The ablation studies are limited, and the paper does not explore the effect of different initialization strategies for the Markov parameters in HOPE-SSM. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other types of models, such as transformers or RNNs, to better contextualize the significance of the proposed method. The implementation details, particularly around the nonuniform sampling of the transfer function and the bilinear transform, are not fully explained, which could hinder reproducibility.

## Questions
1. Could the authors provide a more detailed breakdown of the parameter count for HOPE-SSM compared to S4, S4D, and S5 models to better quantify the efficiency gain?
2. What is the exact training protocol used for HOPE-SSM, including optimizer, learning rate, and batch size, to ensure reproducibility?
3. How does the performance of HOPE-SSM compare to that of transformers or RNNs on the same tasks, and what are the trade-offs in terms of model size and training time?
4. Could the authors provide a perturbation analysis of HOPE-SSM to validate the theoretical claim of numerical stability?
5. How was the sampling period $\Delta t$ chosen for the experiments, and is there a sensitivity analysis showing how different values of $\Delta t$ affect the model's performance and memory retention?

RATING: 8
