## Summary  
This paper introduces scDCA, a drug-conditional adapter for fine-tuning single-cell foundation models (FMs) to predict transcriptional responses to molecular perturbations. The method is designed to address the challenges of data scarcity and modality mismatch by leveraging pre-trained single-cell and molecular FMs. scDCA freezes the original FM weights and injects molecular information via adapter modules, enabling efficient training with less than 1% of the original model parameters. The authors evaluate their approach on four generalization tasks—unseen drugs, unseen drug-cell-line combinations, and both few-shot and zero-shot generalization to new cell lines—using the sciplex3 dataset. They report state-of-the-art performance, particularly in the zero-shot and few-shot settings, and highlight the method’s potential for accelerating drug discovery and biomedical research.

## Strengths  
The paper makes a compelling case for the importance of molecular perturbation prediction in the context of single-cell data, a problem with significant real-world applications in drug discovery and personalized medicine. The proposed scDCA method is novel in its integration of a drug-conditional adapter into a pre-trained single-cell FM, allowing for parameter-efficient fine-tuning while preserving the model’s pre-trained biological knowledge. The evaluation framework is robust, covering a range of generalization scenarios that prior work has not addressed, particularly the zero-shot and few-shot generalization to new cell lines. The results show consistent improvements over existing baselines, especially in these more challenging settings, and the use of R-squared as a metric is appropriate for the regression task. The paper also demonstrates that scDCA’s predictions align with the measurement uncertainty of single-cell data, suggesting practical relevance. The methodology is well-motivated, and the use of pre-trained molecular embeddings (ChemBERTa) is a strong design choice that enhances generalization to unseen drugs.

## Weaknesses  
While the paper presents a promising approach, there are several weaknesses and concerns that need to be addressed. First, the experimental evaluation is limited to only three cell lines and 188 drugs, which may not be sufficient to demonstrate the method’s broad applicability. The lack of testing on independent datasets (e.g., L1000 or Cell Painting) raises concerns about whether the results are generalizable beyond sciplex3. Second, the ablation studies are not comprehensive enough. For instance, the paper does not compare scDCA with other established parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods like LoRA or prefix-tuning, nor does it explore the impact of varying adapter hyperparameters (e.g., bottleneck size, number of adapter layers). Third, the paper lacks statistical significance testing to confirm that the reported improvements over baselines are not due to random variation. Fourth, the reproducibility of the results is hindered by missing implementation details, such as the exact architecture of the adapter module, the hyperparameters used for training, and the method for selecting differentially expressed genes (DEGs). Additionally, the paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the model’s performance on individual genes or the biological relevance of its predictions, which would strengthen the claims of its utility. Finally, the paper’s conclusion is brief and does not clearly summarize the key contributions or results, which weakens the overall impact of the work.

## Questions  
1. How does the performance of scDCA change when using different molecular embedding models (e.g., D-MPNN, MolBERT) instead of ChemBERTa?  
2. What is the exact architecture of the drug-conditional adapter, including the number of layers, activation functions, and how the biases are generated from molecular embeddings?  
3. Could the authors provide a comparison of scDCA with full fine-tuning of the entire scGPT model, including training time, memory usage, and performance?  
4. How do the results change when the model is evaluated on all genes rather than just the top 20 DEGs?  
5. What is the statistical significance of the reported improvements in R-squared metrics compared to the baselines?  
6. How does the model perform when tested on an independent dataset (e.g., L1000 or Cell Painting) to confirm its generalizability beyond sciplex3?  
7. What is the impact of the control gene expression input on the model’s ability to generalize to unseen cell lines, and how does it compare to using other metadata (e.g., cell type labels)?
