## Summary  
The paper introduces VideoWebArena (VideoWA), a benchmark for evaluating the agentic capabilities of long-context multimodal models in tasks involving video understanding. It categorizes tasks into **skill retention** (learning from video demonstrations to perform tasks efficiently) and **factual retention** (extracting specific information from videos to complete tasks). The benchmark includes 2,021 tasks across six domains (Reddit, Classifieds, Shopping, Shopping Admin, GitLab, and Map) and 74 manually crafted video tutorials totaling nearly four hours. The authors evaluate state-of-the-art models like GPT-4o and Gemini 1.5 Pro, finding significant performance gaps compared to human benchmarks. The work highlights the need for improved agentic reasoning in long-context video models and provides a testbed for future research.

## Strengths  
- **Comprehensive Task Taxonomy**: The paper introduces a well-justified taxonomy of video-based agent tasks, distinguishing between skill and factual retention. This dual focus allows for a nuanced evaluation of both procedural learning and information retrieval from video content.  
- **Realistic and Diverse Domains**: The six domains (e.g., Shopping, GitLab, Map) are relevant to real-world applications, and the tasks are grounded in practical workflows. This realism enhances the benchmark's utility for evaluating models in complex, multimodal environments.  
- **Human Performance Baselines**: The inclusion of human performance metrics (73.9% for factual retention tasks and 93.1% for skill retention) provides a clear reference point for model evaluation. The comparison reveals a significant gap, emphasizing the importance of the benchmark.  
- **Intermediate Intent Evaluation**: The use of intermediate intents for factual retention tasks is a novel and valuable contribution. It allows for disentangling video understanding from agentic execution, offering insights into where models fail (e.g., hallucinations, action grounding).  
- **Open-Source Availability**: The authors provide access to the code, benchmark, and documentation at videowebarena.io, which is a strong step toward reproducibility and community adoption.  

## Weaknesses  
- **Insufficient Methodological Detail**: The paper lacks detailed descriptions of the **video creation process**, **task generation protocol**, and **cross-validation methodology**. For example, it is unclear how the 74 videos were annotated, how the 2,021 tasks were selected, or how the intermediate intents were validated for correctness. This hinders reproducibility and limits the ability to assess the benchmark's robustness.  
- **Ambiguous Evaluation Criteria**: The **final and intermediate evaluation functions** are not clearly defined. The paper states that these are imported from existing benchmarks but does not explain how they are adapted for video inputs or what specific metrics are used to determine success (e.g., exact string matching, fuzzy matching, or task completion criteria). This ambiguity makes it difficult to interpret the results or replicate the evaluation.  
- **Missing Figures and Tables**: Several key figures and tables (e.g., Figure \ref{fig:comparison}, Table \ref{table:rewards}) are referenced but not included in the provided text. This omission reduces the clarity of the task taxonomy and evaluation methodology.  
- **Limited Ablation Studies**: The paper does not conduct systematic ablation studies to isolate the impact of specific components (e.g., video summarization, frame sampling rates). The comparison between baseline agents is useful but lacks the depth needed to draw strong conclusions about the effectiveness of different approaches.  
- **Inconsistent Terminology**: The paper alternates between terms like **"LLM agents"** and **"multimodal agents"** without clarifying their relationship or differences. This inconsistency can confuse readers and obscure the paper's contributions.  
- **No Analysis of Failure Modes**: While the paper notes that models struggle with hallucinations and action grounding, it does not provide a **detailed analysis of failure cases** or how they might be addressed. A deeper exploration of these issues would strengthen the paper's contribution to the field.  

## Questions  
- How were the **intermediate intents** for factual retention tasks validated for correctness and relevance? Were there any formal criteria or human annotations used to ensure their quality?  
- What is the **exact process for video creation and annotation**? Were the videos recorded as screen captures of the WebArena/VisualWebArena environments, and if so, how were they aligned with the tasks?  
- How are the **final and intermediate evaluation functions** implemented? Are they based on exact string matching, or do they use more flexible criteria (e.g., semantic similarity)?  
- What **prompting strategies** were used for the baseline agents, particularly the Video Summary Agent? How was the model instructed to extract and summarize video content?  
- Why do **long-context models perform worse with tutorials** than without them? Is this due to the models being overwhelmed by the additional context, or is there a specific issue with how the tutorials are integrated into the agent's decision-making process?  
- How were **human participants selected and evaluated**? Were they naive users or the authors themselves, and were they given the same constraints as the models (e.g., access to the same observation space and action set)?
