### **Summary**

The paper "RePrompt: Prompt Engineering for Large Language Models Agents through Reflection" introduces a novel method called RePrompt for optimizing prompts in LLM agents using intermediate feedback. The method is inspired by a "gradient descent"-like approach and leverages the chat history obtained from interactions and reflections with LLM agents to optimize the step-by-step instructions in the prompts. The paper presents experiments in PDDL generation and travel planning to demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in improving the first-round success rate. The contributions of the paper include the introduction of a summarization-based method to provide specific instructions on how the current prompt could be further improved, and a novel guideline to optimize the prompt in a step-by-step format. The paper also discusses the current limitations of the method and how future work can address them.

### **Strengths**

1. **Novel Methodology**: The paper introduces a novel method, RePrompt, for optimizing prompts in LLM agents using intermediate feedback. This is a significant contribution to the field of LLM agents, as it addresses the challenge of automatic prompt engineering (APE) in scenarios where intermediate feedback is easier to get, and the final evaluation could be expensive, inaccurate, or even missing.

2. **Summarization-Based Approach**: The paper proposes a summarization-based method to give specific instructions on how the current prompt could be further improved. This is a novel and practical approach for real-world LLM agent applications, as it leverages the chat history to provide specific and actionable feedback.

3. **Guideline for Prompt Optimization**: The paper introduces a novel guideline to optimize the prompt in a step-by-step format. This guideline is well-motivated and provides a structured way to avoid overfitting to individual cases.

4. **Experiments in Different Scenarios**: The paper presents experiments in PDDL generation and travel planning to demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in improving the first-round success rate. These experiments are well-designed and provide a good overview of the performance of the method in different scenarios.

5. **Discussion of Limitations**: The paper discusses the current limitations of the method and how future work can address them. This is an important aspect of the paper, as it provides a clear roadmap for future research.

### **Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of Formal Algorithm Description**: The paper does not provide a formal algorithm or pseudocode for RePrompt. This is a major shortcoming for a method that is supposed to be a systematic prompt optimizer. A detailed algorithmic description of RePrompt, including the inputs, outputs, and the exact steps of the summarizer and optimizer LLMs, would help readers understand the flow and how the method is implemented in practice.

2. **Ambiguity in the Role of the Summarizer and Optimizer LLMs**: The paper mentions that the summarizer and optimizer are both LLMs, but it does not clarify whether they are the same model (e.g., GPT-4) or different models. It also does not specify how the summarizer is trained or what its exact task is. Clarifying the model architecture and training procedure for the summarizer and optimizer would help readers understand the method better.

3. **No Explicit Loss Function or Optimization Objective**: The paper draws an analogy to gradient descent but does not define a loss function or optimization objective. In ML, the loss function is the core of the training process, and its absence makes it difficult to assess how the method is actually performing the optimization. Defining a clear objective function or loss metric that the optimizer is trying to minimize or maximize would strengthen the methodology.

4. **Limited Discussion of Prompt Structure and Format**: The paper mentions that RePrompt focuses on the step-by-step instruction phase of the prompt but does not provide a concrete example of what the original prompt looks like or how it is modified. Providing a concrete example of a prompt before and after RePrompt optimization would help readers understand the types of changes being made and how the method interacts with the structure of the prompt.

5. **No Mention of Prompt Initialization or Baseline Prompts**: The paper states that RePrompt starts with the original prompt but does not specify what the original prompt is in the PDDL and TravelPlanner tasks. This is important for reproducibility and for understanding the baseline from which improvements are made. Including the exact original prompts used in the experiments would be essential for replication and for evaluating the effectiveness of the method.

6. **No Discussion of How the Optimizer Handles Conflicting Feedback**: The paper does not address how the optimizer LLM handles conflicting or ambiguous feedback from the chat history. In real-world scenarios, the chat history may contain contradictory suggestions, and it is unclear how RePrompt resolves these. Discussing how the optimizer LLM handles conflicting feedback and whether it uses any mechanism to prioritize or weight different types of feedback would be helpful.

7. **No Mention of Prompt Length or Complexity Constraints**: The paper does not mention any constraints on the length or complexity of the optimized prompts. Prompt engineering is often sensitive to prompt length, and it is unclear whether RePrompt is designed to keep the prompt concise or if it allows for arbitrarily long prompts. Including a discussion of how the method manages prompt length and complexity, and whether there are any constraints or optimizations in place to prevent the prompt from becoming too long or unwieldy, would be helpful.

8. **Small Training Set in TravelPlanner**: In the TravelPlanner experiment, the paper uses only 10 data points from the validation set as the training set for RePrompt. This is a very small sample size and may not be sufficient to capture the diversity of the task. Justifying the use of such a small training set and considering using a larger subset (e.g., 50 or 100 data points) to ensure that the method is not overfitting to a small number of examples would be helpful. Additionally, reporting results on a held-out test set to assess generalization would be important.

9. **No Clear Definition of the Evaluation Metrics**: The paper mentions metrics like "final pass rate," "delivery rate," and "commonsense pass rate," but it does not provide a clear definition of these metrics or how they are calculated. Defining all evaluation metrics in detail, including how they are derived from the chat history and the final output, would be essential for readers to understand the results and for reproducibility.

10. **No Comparison with Manual Prompt Engineering**: The paper does not compare RePrompt with manually engineered prompts, which is a common baseline in prompt engineering research. This makes it difficult to assess whether the method is truly effective or just marginally better than a human-designed prompt. Including a comparison with manually engineered prompts as a baseline would help demonstrate the value of the method in real-world applications.

11. **No Ablation on the Summarizer and Optimizer Models**: The paper does not perform an ablation study on the choice of summarizer and optimizer models. It is unclear whether the results are due to the method itself or the specific models used (e.g., GPT-4). Conducting an ablation study on the choice of summarizer and optimizer models would help understand the contribution of each part of the method.

12. **No Analysis of the Impact of the Number of Iterations**: The paper mentions that RePrompt is trained for 1 or 5 epochs, but it does not provide a detailed analysis of how the number of iterations affects the performance of the method. Including a more detailed analysis of the impact of the number of iterations on the performance of RePrompt would be helpful. For example, plotting the success rate as a function of the number of epochs to show the convergence behavior of the method would be useful.

13. **No Mention of Hyperparameters**: The paper mentions using a temperature of 0 and a seed of 42, but it does not discuss other hyperparameters that may affect the performance of the method, such as the number of tokens in the prompt, the number of suggestions generated by the optimizer, or the length of the chat history used for summarization. Including a discussion of all hyperparameters used in the experiments and considering performing an ablation study on them to understand their impact on the results would be helpful.

14. **No Cross-Validation or Multiple Runs**: The paper does not mention whether the experiments were run multiple times or whether cross-validation was used to assess the robustness of the results. Reporting results from multiple runs of the experiments and using cross-validation to assess the robustness of the method would be important, especially for LLM-based methods, which can be highly variable due to sampling and model behavior.

15. **No Analysis of the Cost of the Method**: The paper mentions that the feedback in PDDL is expensive, but it does not provide a detailed analysis of the cost of using RePrompt compared to other methods. This is important for understanding the practicality of the method. Including a cost analysis of the method, including the number of API calls, the time taken for each iteration, and the total cost of the experiments, would help readers understand the trade-offs between performance and cost.

16. **Limited Scope of Ablation Study**: The ablation study is limited to the multiplication of the number of training samples and epochs. It does not explore other important aspects of the method, such as the impact of the summarizer, the optimizer, or the specific rules used for prompt improvement. Expanding the ablation study to include more aspects of the method would help readers understand which components of the method are most critical for performance.

17. **No Comparison with Variants of RePrompt**: The paper does not compare RePrompt with variants of the method, such as a version that does not use the summarizer or a version that uses a different set of rules for prompt improvement. Including a comparison with variants of RePrompt to understand the contribution of each component would be helpful. For example, testing a version of RePrompt that does not use the summarizer and instead directly uses the chat history for prompt optimization would be useful.

18. **No Analysis of the Impact of the Auxiliary Checker**: The paper mentions an auxiliary checker used to convert prompts into a structured, step-by-step format when needed, but it does not analyze the impact of this component on the final results. Including an ablation study on the use of the auxiliary checker would be helpful. For example, testing whether the method performs better with or without the auxiliary checker and whether the checker is necessary for certain types of prompts would be useful.

19. **No Analysis of the Impact of the Pre-Encoded Solutions**: The paper mentions that some frequently used solutions are pre-encoded in the optimizer prompt, but it does not analyze the impact of this design choice on the performance of the method. Including an ablation study on the use of pre-encoded solutions would be helpful. For example, testing whether the method performs better with or without the pre-encoded solutions and whether the number of pre-encoded solutions affects the results would be useful.

20. **No Analysis of the Impact of the Feedback Generator**: The paper does not analyze the impact of the feedback generator on the performance of RePrompt. It is unclear whether the method is robust to different types of feedback generators or if it is highly dependent on the specific feedback used. Including an ablation study on the feedback generator would be helpful. For example, testing whether the method performs better with different feedback generators (e.g., REACT vs. Reflexion) and whether the quality of the feedback affects the results would be useful.

21. **Informal and Lack of Quantitative Analysis in Error Analysis**: The error analysis section is well-motivated and provides insight into the limitations of the method. However, it is somewhat informal and lacks a structured approach to analyzing the errors. Including a quantitative analysis of the errors, such as the percentage of prompts that are incomplete or the percentage of output format errors, would help readers understand the severity of the issues and how well the method addresses them.

22. **No Comparison with Other Methods in Error Handling**: The paper does not compare how RePrompt handles errors compared to other prompt engineering methods or baselines. Including a comparison of error handling between RePrompt and other methods would be helpful. For example, testing whether RePrompt reduces the number of errors compared to PromptAgent or other APE methods would be useful.

23. **No Discussion of Error Types in the TravelPlanner Domain**: The paper mentions that RePrompt does not help with hallucination errors in the TravelPlanner domain, but it does not provide a detailed breakdown of the types of hallucinations or how they could be addressed. Including a detailed breakdown of the hallucination errors and discussing potential ways to address them within the RePrompt framework would be helpful. For example, could the summarizer be modified to detect and address hallucinations?

24. **No Discussion of Error Propagation**: The paper does not discuss how errors in the chat history or the summarizer could propagate to the final prompt and affect the performance of the LLM agent. Including a discussion of error propagation and how the method is designed to be robust to errors in the chat history or summarizer output would be helpful.
