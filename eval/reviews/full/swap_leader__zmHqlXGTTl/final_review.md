## Summary  
The paper introduces a new task, LayoutSciPG, for the automatic generation of scientific posters from academic papers. It presents a large-scale dataset, SciPG, containing over 10,000 paper-poster pairs, and proposes a multimodal extractor-generator framework to address the challenges of content extraction, paraphrasing, and layout generation. The framework includes a Multimodal Document Extractor (MDE) for extracting key text and image elements and an Interactive Generator (IG) with an adaptive memory mechanism to manage long-term dependencies and GPU memory constraints. The paper evaluates the proposed method using both automatic and human evaluation metrics, showing improvements over existing baselines in content extraction, image selection, and layout prediction. However, the paper also highlights remaining challenges, particularly in achieving aesthetically pleasing layouts.

## Strengths  
The paper makes several notable contributions to the field of scientific poster generation. First, the creation of the SciPG dataset is a significant step forward, as it is the first large-scale, publicly available dataset that includes both paper-poster pairs and element-level alignments. This dataset will likely enable future research in this area. Second, the proposed multimodal extractor-generator framework is well-designed and addresses the limitations of prior work, which often focused on either content extraction or layout design in isolation. The integration of both tasks into a unified model is a novel and promising approach. Third, the paper introduces an adaptive memory mechanism in the generator, which effectively handles long-term dependencies and reduces GPU memory consumption, a critical issue in processing lengthy scientific documents and generating complex poster layouts. The experimental results, including ablation studies and human evaluations, provide strong evidence of the method's effectiveness, particularly in improving image extraction and layout metrics.

## Weaknesses  
While the paper presents a compelling framework and dataset, there are several weaknesses and concerns. The methodology section, though detailed, is somewhat dense and could benefit from clearer explanations and more intuitive examples, especially for the adaptive memory mechanism and the pre-training objectives. The paper also lacks a thorough discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach, particularly in the area of layout aesthetics, where both the proposed method and the baseline received relatively low human evaluation scores. Additionally, the paper does not provide sufficient detail on the dataset collection and annotation process, which is essential for reproducibility. For instance, the criteria for selecting papers and posters, the alignment process, and the preprocessing steps are not clearly described. The implementation details, such as hyperparameters and training procedures, are also insufficiently detailed, making it difficult to fully assess the reproducibility of the results. Finally, the related work section is somewhat disconnected from the proposed solution and could be better integrated to highlight how the new approach advances the field.

## Questions  
1. Could the authors provide a more detailed description of the dataset collection and annotation process, including the criteria for selecting papers and posters and the method for aligning elements between them?  
2. What specific preprocessing steps were applied to the text and image elements in the dataset, and how were the bounding box coordinates normalized or standardized?  
3. The paper mentions the use of RoBERTa and BART as pre-trained models. Could the authors elaborate on how these models were fine-tuned for the specific task of scientific poster generation, and whether any domain-specific adaptations were made?  
4. The human evaluation results indicate that layout aesthetics remain a challenge. Could the authors provide examples of posters where the model failed to produce a visually appealing layout, and what specific aspects of the layout were problematic?  
5. The paper does not discuss the computational cost of training the proposed framework. Could the authors provide more information on the training time, GPU memory usage, and any optimizations used to make the model scalable?
