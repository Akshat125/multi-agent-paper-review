## Summary
The paper "Agree to Disagree: Demystifying Homogeneous Deep Ensembles through Distributional Equivalence" challenges the conventional understanding of deep ensembles, which attributes their effectiveness to the Jensen gap. Instead, it proposes a new theoretical framework based on the distributional equivalence property of homogeneous models. The authors argue that the predictions of independently trained models, despite point-wise differences, form identical distributions over the data space. This insight leads to rigorous proofs of the ensemble's effectiveness and a method to estimate performance using only two models. The paper is supported by extensive empirical validation across multiple datasets and model architectures, offering a fresh perspective on the mechanisms behind deep ensembles.

## Strengths
The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by introducing the concept of distributional equivalence, which provides a more accurate explanation for the effectiveness of deep ensembles than the Jensen gap. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with well-structured proofs and derivations that are supported by empirical results. The authors also propose a practical scheme to estimate the asymptotic performance of ensembles using only two models, which is a novel and impactful contribution for practitioners. The experimental setup is comprehensive, covering a variety of datasets (CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, TinyImagenet) and model architectures (CNNs and ResNets), and the results are well-aligned with the theoretical predictions. The paper also addresses the long-standing debate between the effectiveness of deep ensembles and large single models, offering a new perspective on their differing mechanisms.

## Weaknesses
While the paper is well-written and the theoretical analysis is sound, there are a few areas that could be improved. The paper assumes a high level of mathematical and statistical knowledge, which may limit its accessibility to a broader machine learning audience. Some key concepts, such as "neural collapse" and "distributional equivalence," are introduced without sufficient intuitive explanation, making it harder for readers to grasp their significance. Additionally, the paper could benefit from more explicit discussion of the limitations of its theoretical framework, particularly in terms of generalizability to non-convex loss functions or other types of tasks beyond classification. The experimental section, while thorough, lacks specific details on hyperparameters and training procedures, which are essential for full reproducibility. Finally, the paper could be strengthened by including comparisons with other ensemble methods, such as bagging or boosting, to better contextualize the relative advantages of the proposed approach.

## Questions
1. The paper assumes the distributional equivalence property is an observational conclusion. Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation or theoretical justification for why this property holds across different models and datasets?
2. The paper mentions the use of the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test and Wasserstein distance to validate the distributional equivalence property. Could the authors provide more details on the specific thresholds or criteria used to determine statistical significance in these tests?
3. The paper discusses the trade-off between increasing the number of ensemble members and scaling up a single model. Could the authors provide a more direct comparison of the computational costs and performance gains of these two approaches on the tested datasets?
4. The paper focuses on classification tasks and convex loss functions. Could the authors comment on the potential applicability of their framework to regression tasks or non-convex loss functions?
