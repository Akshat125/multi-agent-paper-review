## Summary
The paper "Ref-EMGBench: Benchmarking Reference Normalization for Electromyography Data" presents a systematic evaluation of five amplitude-based normalization techniques for EMG data in the context of hand gesture recognition. The study uses a leave-one-subject-out train-test split and three publicly available datasets to assess how these methods reduce intersubject variability and improve classification accuracy. The authors find that Min-Max and Peak normalization outperform the other methods, and they provide visualizations using t-SNE and Wasserstein distance matrices to illustrate the reduction in domain shift. The paper also highlights the benefits of inter-subject normalization over intra-subject normalization, suggesting that leveraging variability between subjects can enhance model generalization. The code for the benchmark is made publicly available, which is a positive step for reproducibility.

## Strengths
- The paper addresses a critical issue in EMG-based gesture recognition: intersubject variability and domain shift. This is a well-motivated problem with clear applications in prosthetics, rehabilitation, and human-robot interaction.
- The methodology is well-structured, with a clear experimental setup using a leave-one-subject-out approach. The use of three diverse datasets (CapgMyo DBb, NinaPro DB3, and DB5) ensures a robust evaluation across different recording conditions and user groups.
- The paper provides both quantitative and qualitative analyses of the normalization methods. The use of metrics such as accuracy, AUROC, MMD, and KL divergence, along with t-SNE and Wasserstein distance visualizations, offers a comprehensive view of the performance and domain shift reduction.
- The findings are significant and actionable. The identification of Min-Max and Peak normalization as superior methods, along with the insight that inter-subject normalization outperforms intra-subject normalization, provides a clear direction for future research and practical applications.
- The paper is well-written and organized, with a clear introduction, methodology, and results section. The contributions are clearly stated and supported by the experimental findings.

## Weaknesses
- The paper lacks sufficient detail on the model architecture and training procedure. While it mentions using ResNet18, it does not provide specifics on the implementation, such as the exact configuration of the model or the training hyperparameters beyond the learning rate and number of epochs. This omission hinders reproducibility and makes it difficult to assess the model's behavior.
- The data preprocessing steps, particularly the conversion of EMG signals into heatmap images, are not clearly explained. This is a critical step in the pipeline, and without a detailed description, it is challenging to understand how the normalization affects the model's input and performance.
- The ablation studies are limited in scope. While the paper compares intra-subject and inter-subject normalization, it does not explore the impact of normalization on different layers of the model or compare the benchmarked methods with other normalization strategies (e.g., frequency-based normalization). A more comprehensive ablation would strengthen the paper's claims.
- The paper does not include statistical significance tests to determine whether the observed differences in performance between normalization methods are meaningful. This omission makes it difficult to assess the reliability of the results and the robustness of the conclusions.
- The code availability is mentioned, but the paper does not provide a direct link to the code or specify the exact versions of the libraries and frameworks used. This lack of detail could hinder reproducibility and make it harder for others to replicate the experiments.

## Questions
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how the EMG signals are converted into heatmap images, and how this conversion is affected by the normalization methods used?
- What is the theoretical basis for the selection of the five normalization methods, and why were other popular normalization techniques (e.g., median-based or percentile-based normalization) not included in the benchmark?
- How do the authors ensure that the results are not influenced by the specific choice of reference subject in the inter-subject normalization setting? Is there a per-subject breakdown of performance to assess robustness?
- Could the authors include a comparison with a baseline where no normalization is applied, to better highlight the importance of normalization in reducing domain shift and improving classification accuracy?
- What steps were taken to ensure the reproducibility of the experiments, such as fixing random seeds or using cross-validation? Could the authors provide standard deviations or confidence intervals for the reported metrics?

RATING: 8
