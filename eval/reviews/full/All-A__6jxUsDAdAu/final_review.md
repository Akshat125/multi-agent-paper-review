## Summary  
This paper investigates the phenomenon of benign overfitting in the context of out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization for over-parameterized linear models under covariate shift. It provides non-asymptotic excess risk bounds for ridge regression and Principal Component Regression (PCR), showing that benign overfitting can occur in the OOD regime when the target covariance matrix satisfies certain structural conditions. The analysis identifies key quantities relating source and target covariances that govern performance, and the results are sharp, recovering prior in-distribution and under-parameterized bounds as special cases. The paper also demonstrates that PCR achieves a faster statistical rate ($\mathcal{O}(1/n)$) than ridge regression ($\mathcal{O}(1/\sqrt{n})$) in scenarios with large shifts in minor directions, provided the signal lies in the major directions of the source. Simulation experiments support these theoretical findings.  

## Strengths  
The paper makes a clear and novel contribution to the theoretical understanding of OOD generalization in over-parameterized models. It extends the concept of benign overfitting beyond the in-distribution setting, addressing a critical gap in the literature. The derived bounds are instance-dependent and non-asymptotic, which is a significant improvement over many existing results that rely on asymptotic or generic assumptions. The analysis is rigorous, with careful decomposition of bias and variance terms under covariate shift, and the results are shown to recover prior work in specific cases, demonstrating their generality.  

The comparison between ridge regression and PCR is insightful, particularly in highlighting PCR’s advantages when the target distribution has significant variance in the minor directions of the source. The simulation experiments are well-structured and validate the theoretical claims, showing the expected scaling of excess risk with sample size $n$. The paper also situates its work within the broader context of distribution shift and over-parameterization, providing a useful reference to related literature.  

## Weaknesses  
The paper’s clarity and reproducibility could be improved. Mathematical notations such as $\mathcal{T}$, $\mathcal{U}$, and $\mathcal{V}$ are introduced without sufficient explanation, making it harder for readers to grasp their role in the analysis. The definitions of effective rank $r_k$ and $R_k$ are not clearly tied to the practical implications of the results. Additionally, the simulation experiments lack detailed descriptions of the data generation process, hyperparameter choices, and evaluation metrics, which hinders reproducibility. For example, the exact values of eigenvalues used in the experiments and the method for selecting principal components in PCR are not specified.  

The experimental section could also benefit from a more explicit comparison with the in-distribution case as a baseline. While the paper mentions that PCR achieves a faster rate in certain OOD scenarios, it does not explore the trade-offs or limitations of PCR in other settings, such as when the signal is not concentrated in the major directions. The paper’s conclusion is brief and could be expanded to better contextualize the results and their broader implications for machine learning.  

## Questions  
1. The paper states that PCR achieves a faster rate than ridge regression when the target covariance has significant variance in the minor directions. What happens if the signal is not concentrated in the major directions of the source? Does PCR still outperform ridge regression in such cases?  
2. The theoretical bounds depend on the condition number $L$ of the source covariance matrix. How sensitive are the results to large values of $L$, and does the paper provide any guidance on how to handle ill-conditioned source covariances in practice?  
3. The simulation experiments mention varying $\|\mathcal{T}\|$ and $\mathrm{tr}[\mathcal{U}]/\mathrm{tr}[\mathcal{V}]$, but the exact values of the eigenvalues $\lambda_j$ for the source and target covariance matrices are not specified. Could the authors provide a concrete example of the data generation process used in the experiments?  
4. The paper claims that its bounds are sharp and recover prior results in the in-distribution and under-parameterized settings. Could the authors explicitly compare their bounds with those of Tsigler & Bartlett (2023) and Ge et al. (2024) in terms of constants and assumptions?  
5. The paper briefly mentions non-parametric OOD generalization but does not connect it to the linear model analysis. How do the results for linear models relate to the broader non-parametric literature on OOD generalization?  

RATING: 8
