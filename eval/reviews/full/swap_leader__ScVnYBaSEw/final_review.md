## Summary  
The paper introduces **UNSURE**, a self-supervised learning method for image reconstruction that does not require explicit knowledge of the noise level. It addresses the robustness-expressivity trade-off in self-supervised learning by proposing a theoretical framework that characterizes estimators based on constraints on their (expected) derivatives. The authors show that cross-validation methods (e.g., Noise2Self) impose overly restrictive constraints, while SURE-based methods are optimal but brittle to noise-level misspecification. UNSURE bridges this gap by enforcing zero expected divergence (ZED) in the estimator, enabling it to learn from noisy data without prior noise-level information. The method is extended to handle spatially correlated Gaussian noise, Poisson-Gaussian noise, and exponential family distributions. Experiments on Gaussian denoising (MNIST, DIV2K), tomography (LIDC), and accelerated MRI (FastMRI) demonstrate that UNSURE achieves near-state-of-the-art performance compared to both self-supervised and supervised baselines, particularly in settings with unknown noise levels or correlations.

## Strengths  
- **Theoretical Insight**: The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the robustness-expressivity trade-off in self-supervised learning. It clearly links the performance of estimators to the constraints on their derivatives, offering a novel perspective on why cross-validation methods are suboptimal and how SURE-based methods can be generalized to unknown noise levels.  
- **Novel Method**: The proposed UNSURE method is a significant advancement. By relaxing the zero-derivative constraint of cross-validation to a zero-expected divergence constraint, it achieves better performance while remaining robust to noise-level misspecification. The closed-form solution for the Lagrange multipliers is particularly elegant and practical.  
- **Comprehensive Experiments**: The experiments span a wide range of imaging inverse problems (denoising, tomography, MRI) and noise models (Gaussian, Poisson-Gaussian, correlated noise). The results are compared with multiple state-of-the-art self-supervised methods (e.g., Noise2Score, Neighbor2Neighbor, SURE) and supervised learning, showing that UNSURE consistently outperforms or matches existing approaches.  
- **Generalizability**: The method is extended to handle spatially correlated noise and Poisson-Gaussian noise, which are common in real-world imaging applications. The authors also provide a general formulation for inverse problems with incomplete measurements, broadening the scope of the approach.  
- **Clear Contributions**: The paper explicitly lists its contributions, including the theoretical framework, the new self-supervised objective, and empirical validation across diverse tasks. These are well-supported by the analysis and experiments.

## Weaknesses  
- **Limited Practical Noise-Level Estimation Details**: While the paper claims to provide closed-form formulas for noise-level estimation, the details of how these are computed in practice (e.g., for Poisson-Gaussian noise) are deferred to the appendix. This makes it harder to assess the method’s robustness to real-world noise-level variations without further reading.  
- **Computational Overhead**: The method requires additional training steps (e.g., alternating optimization of the estimator and Lagrange multipliers), which increases computational cost compared to supervised learning. The paper does not thoroughly analyze the trade-off between performance gains and computational costs.  
- **Ablation on Kernel Size**: The ablation study on kernel size in the correlated noise experiment shows that overspecifying the kernel size can improve performance. However, the paper does not explore how this sensitivity might affect broader applicability or whether adaptive kernel estimation could be integrated.  
- **Lack of Perceptual Evaluation**: The paper focuses on PSNR as the primary evaluation metric. While PSNR is standard, it does not capture perceptual quality. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not propose or test alternative metrics (e.g., SSIM, perceptual loss) that might better reflect real-world utility.  
- **Reproducibility Gaps**: The experimental setup lacks sufficient detail for full reproducibility. For example, the U-Net architecture, training hyperparameters (e.g., batch size, learning rate schedule), and preprocessing steps for datasets like FastMRI and LIDC are not explicitly described in the main text.  

## Questions  
1. How does the method perform when the noise distribution is not in the exponential family or when the assumed noise model is misspecified? The paper focuses on Gaussian and Poisson-Gaussian noise, but real-world noise can be more complex.  
2. What is the computational overhead of the proposed method compared to standard SURE and cross-validation approaches? The paper mentions increased training cost but does not quantify it.  
3. Can the method be adapted to learn perceptual or distortion-robust metrics (e.g., SSIM, perceptual loss) instead of the $\ell_2$ loss? The authors note this as a limitation but do not explore it further.  
4. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the Monte Carlo approximation constant $\tau$ in the divergence estimation? The paper uses $\tau = 0.01$ but does not evaluate its impact.  
5. Does the proposed method scale to high-resolution images or large-scale datasets? The experiments use relatively small datasets (e.g., 900 patches for DIV2K), and the scalability is not discussed.  

RATING: 8
