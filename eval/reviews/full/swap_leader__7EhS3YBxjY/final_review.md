## Summary
The paper introduces MIA-Bench, a new benchmark for evaluating the strict instruction adherence of multimodal large language models (MLLMs). It consists of 400 image-prompt pairs with diverse instruction categories, including description, length limit, mention, genre, grammar, math, perspective, and OCR. The authors evaluate 29 state-of-the-art MLLMs on MIA-Bench, revealing significant performance variations. They also propose supervised fine-tuning (SFT) using additional training data to improve instruction adherence, showing promising results with LLaVA-NeXT-13b. The paper positions MIA-Bench as a novel and impactful tool for assessing and advancing MLLM capabilities in precise instruction following.

## Strengths
The paper makes a compelling case for the need of a benchmark focused on strict instruction adherence in MLLMs, a capability that is underexplored in the current literature. MIA-Bench is well-justified as a novel contribution, with a clear distinction from existing benchmarks that either focus on fixed-form VQA or open-ended conversations without rigorous adherence criteria. The benchmark's design is thoughtful, with a diverse set of image sources and a structured approach to instruction composition. The evaluation methodology, particularly the use of GPT-4o as a judge, is innovative and provides a transparent, reproducible way to assess sub-instruction compliance. The results are comprehensive, covering both closed-source and open-source models, and the SFT experiments demonstrate a practical path for improving instruction adherence without significant degradation on other tasks. The paper also highlights the importance of instruction fidelity for real-world applications, which adds to its relevance and potential impact.

## Weaknesses
While the paper presents a strong foundation for MIA-Bench, there are several areas that could be improved. First, the abstract and introduction lack a clear definition of "instruction adherence" and the specific evaluation criteria used to measure it. This ambiguity could hinder the reader's understanding of the benchmark's purpose and how it differs from existing ones. Second, the methodology section does not provide sufficient detail on the weighting system for sub-instructions or the exact prompts used in the SFT data construction, which limits reproducibility. Third, the experimental section could benefit from ablation studies to better understand the factors contributing to the SFT improvements, such as the number of training epochs or the composition of the training data. Additionally, the paper does not explore the generalization of SFT improvements to other benchmarks or real-world tasks, which would strengthen the argument for its practical utility. Finally, while the use of GPT-4o as a judge is well-justified, the paper could include more validation from other models or human evaluators to address potential bias concerns.

## Questions
1. Could the authors provide a more explicit definition of "instruction adherence" and the specific criteria used to evaluate it in the introduction?
2. How were the weights for sub-instructions determined, and what is the rationale for prioritizing the "description" category in the scoring system?
3. What are the exact prompts or methods used to generate the additional SFT data, and how were the hyperparameters for the training process selected?
4. Could the authors include ablation studies to isolate the impact of different factors in the SFT process, such as the number of training epochs or the size of the training data?
5. How do the SFT improvements generalize to other benchmarks or real-world tasks, and what evidence supports this generalization?

RATING: 8
