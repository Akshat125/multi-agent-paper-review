## Summary
The paper introduces UNSURE, a self-supervised learning method for image reconstruction that extends Stein's Unbiased Risk Estimator (SURE) to handle unknown noise levels. It presents a theoretical framework to understand the expressivity-robustness trade-off in self-supervised learning and demonstrates the method's effectiveness across various inverse problems, including Gaussian denoising, colored Gaussian noise, Poisson-Gaussian noise in tomography, and accelerated MRI. The proposed method is shown to outperform existing self-supervised approaches and achieve performance close to supervised learning in these tasks.

## Strengths
The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by characterizing the expressivity-robustness trade-off in self-supervised learning through the lens of derivative constraints on the estimator. This framework provides a clear understanding of how different assumptions about the noise distribution affect the performance of the learned estimator. The proposed method, UNSURE, is a novel extension of SURE that does not require explicit knowledge of the noise level, making it more practical for real-world applications where such information is often unavailable.

The experiments are well-designed and cover a range of relevant imaging inverse problems. The results show that the proposed method performs competitively with supervised learning and outperforms other self-supervised baselines in most cases. For example, in the colored Gaussian noise experiment on the DIV2K dataset, the proposed method achieves a PSNR of 28.72 dB, which is close to the 29.77 dB achieved by the SURE method with known noise covariance. Similarly, in the Poisson-Gaussian tomography experiment on the LIDC dataset, the proposed method achieves a PSNR of 33.31 dB, which is close to the 33.76 dB achieved by the SURE method with known noise levels.

The paper also provides a clear comparison between the proposed method and existing approaches, such as Noise2Self, Neighbor2Neighbor, and SURE. The theoretical analysis of the ZED constraint and its relationship to the MMSE estimator is insightful and contributes to a deeper understanding of self-supervised learning in image reconstruction. The ablation study on kernel size in the colored noise experiment is informative and shows that the method is not overly sensitive to this hyperparameter.

## Weaknesses
While the paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded method, there are several areas where the contribution could be more clearly demonstrated or where the experimental evaluation could be strengthened. One key issue is the lack of comparison with methods that explicitly estimate the noise level during training, such as~\cite{kim_noise_2022}. This omission is significant because one of the main contributions of the paper is the ability to handle unknown noise levels without requiring additional estimation steps.

The experiments are well-structured but could benefit from more detailed analysis. For example, the MNIST denoising experiment uses a relatively simple dataset and may not fully reflect the method's performance in more complex or high-resolution settings. The paper should consider testing the method on more challenging datasets, such as CIFAR-10 or high-resolution medical images, to better assess its generalizability.

The paper also does not provide a detailed explanation of how the proposed method generalizes to non-Gaussian noise models, such as Poisson-Gaussian and exponential family noise. While the method is described for these cases, the theoretical justification is less detailed compared to the Gaussian case. A more thorough derivation or justification for these generalizations would improve the clarity and generalizability of the method.

Additionally, the paper mentions that the proposed method is more computationally intensive than supervised learning due to the additional evaluation of the estimator during training. However, it does not quantify this overhead or compare it with the performance gains, which is important for assessing the practicality of the method in real-world applications.

## Questions
1. How does the proposed theoretical framework for the expressivity-robustness trade-off differ from or build upon existing theoretical work in the field? Is this trade-off a new concept, or has it been previously discussed in the literature?
2. Are the generalizations of UNSURE to Poisson-Gaussian and exponential family noise models novel, or have similar approaches been proposed in the literature? How does the proposed method compare to existing methods in these settings?
3. The paper mentions that the proposed method is more computationally intensive than supervised learning. Could the authors provide a quantitative comparison of the computational overhead and the performance gains to justify the trade-off in practical applications?
4. The paper does not compare with methods that estimate the noise level during training, such as~\cite{kim_noise_2022}. How does the proposed method compare to these approaches in terms of performance and robustness to noise level misspecification?
