## Summary  
This paper investigates the phenomenon of benign overfitting in the context of out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization for over-parameterized linear models. It provides non-asymptotic theoretical guarantees for ridge regression and Principal Component Regression (PCR) under covariate shift, where the source and target distributions differ in their covariance structure. The authors identify key quantities relating the source and target covariances that govern OOD performance and show that ridge regression achieves a fast $ \mathcal{O}(1/n) $ excess risk rate when the target covariance’s minor directions are well-controlled. In contrast, when the target covariance has large shifts in minor directions, ridge regression incurs a slower $ \mathcal{O}(1/\sqrt{n}) $ rate, while PCR maintains the fast $ \mathcal{O}(1/n) $ rate under the same conditions. Simulation experiments validate these theoretical findings, demonstrating the practical relevance of the results. The paper bridges a gap in understanding how benign overfitting extends to OOD settings and highlights PCR as a robust alternative in challenging scenarios.

## Strengths  
- The paper makes a **clear and novel contribution** by extending the benign overfitting framework to OOD generalization, a critical problem in modern machine learning. This is a significant departure from prior work, which has largely focused on in-distribution or under-parameterized models.  
- The **theoretical analysis is rigorous and sharp**, with non-asymptotic bounds that recover prior results in both in-distribution and under-parameterized settings. The authors carefully define structural conditions on the source and target covariances and derive bounds that are both interpretable and mathematically sound.  
- The **comparison between ridge regression and PCR** is insightful. The paper demonstrates that PCR can achieve a fast $ \mathcal{O}(1/n) $ rate even when ridge regression fails, offering a practical alternative for OOD scenarios with large shifts in minor directions.  
- The **simulation experiments are well-designed** and align with the theoretical claims. The results confirm the predicted rates for both ridge regression and PCR, and the visualizations (e.g., Figure 1) effectively illustrate the impact of covariate shifts in major and minor directions.  
- The paper **articulates its contributions clearly**, including a structured list in the abstract and a detailed discussion in the introduction. This helps readers quickly grasp the scope and novelty of the work.  

## Weaknesses  
- The **notation and definitions are dense and occasionally unclear**, particularly in the introduction and abstract. Terms like “effective rank” and key quantities (e.g., $ \mathcal{T}, \mathcal{U}, \mathcal{V} $) are introduced without sufficient explanation, which may hinder readability for a broader audience. A glossary or more intuitive explanations would improve clarity.  
- The **assumptions (e.g., CondNum)** are not fully justified in terms of their practical relevance. While the paper states that these assumptions are standard in the literature, it does not elaborate on how likely they are to hold in real-world data or how they might be relaxed.  
- The **experimental section is limited in scope**. The simulations focus on synthetic data with specific covariance structures, but the paper does not test the proposed methods on real-world datasets or more complex models (e.g., non-linear models). This limits the generalizability of the empirical claims.  
- The **discussion of practical implications is brief**. The paper could have expanded on how the theoretical findings translate to real-world applications, such as domain adaptation or robustness in high-dimensional settings. For example, it does not address how to estimate the key quantities $ \mathcal{T}, \mathcal{U}, \mathcal{V} $ from data or how to choose $ k $, the number of major directions.  
- The **proofs and derivations in the appendix are highly technical and lack intuition**, making it difficult for readers to follow the reasoning without significant background in high-dimensional statistics. More illustrative examples or visualizations of the theoretical bounds would strengthen the paper.  

## Questions  
1. How can the key quantities $ \mathcal{T}, \mathcal{U}, \mathcal{V} $ be estimated in practice, given that the true source and target covariances are unknown?  
2. The paper assumes a fixed number of major directions $ k $. What happens if $ k $ is misspecified or estimated from the data? How does this affect the excess risk bounds?  
3. The simulations use synthetic data with controlled covariance structures. Have the authors tested their theoretical claims on real-world datasets with natural covariate shifts? If not, what are the challenges in doing so?  
4. The paper highlights PCR as a better alternative in certain OOD scenarios. What are the computational and practical trade-offs of using PCR over ridge regression in high-dimensional settings?  
5. The analysis focuses on linear models. Can the insights about benign overfitting in OOD settings be extended to non-linear models, such as neural networks? If so, what are the key challenges?
