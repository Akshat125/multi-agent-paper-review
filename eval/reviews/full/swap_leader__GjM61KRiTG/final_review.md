## Summary  
The paper introduces **Bi-Factorial Preference Optimization (Bi-FAPO)**, a supervised learning framework that re-parameterizes the multi-objective RLHF problem of balancing safety and helpfulness in large language models (LLMs) into a single supervised learning objective. The method leverages a labeling function to capture global preference rankings, enabling the model to optimize both objectives simultaneously. The authors demonstrate that their approach is theoretically equivalent to multi-objective RLHF and show that it outperforms existing methods in both helpfulness and harmlessness metrics. Notably, Bi-FAPO eliminates the need for human prompting and annotation, achieving strong results with minimal computational resources and public datasets. The paper also includes ablation studies and comparisons to baselines, highlighting the efficiency and effectiveness of the proposed method.  

## Strengths  
- The paper presents a **novel and theoretically grounded approach** to align LLMs with both helpfulness and safety objectives. The derivation of the supervised learning objective and the proof of its equivalence to multi-objective RLHF are rigorous and well-explained.  
- The **empirical results are compelling**, showing that Bi-FAPO significantly improves harmlessness scores (e.g., 77.16% average harmlessness) while maintaining or even improving helpfulness compared to methods that focus on a single objective. The comparison with DPO, IPO, and MORL demonstrates the method's superiority in balancing these conflicting goals.  
- The **efficiency of the method is a major strength**. The authors show that Bi-FAPO achieves results comparable to labor-intensive RLHF methods (e.g., Beaver and Llama2) using only 30K data points and a single training iteration, with no human annotation required. This is a significant reduction in both computational cost and human effort.  
- The **ablation studies** provide insight into the role of key components like the shift parameter $\alpha$ and buffered training, showing their impact on performance. These experiments help validate the design choices and highlight the importance of balancing the two objectives.  
- The **benchmarking and evaluation setup** is comprehensive, including both discriminative and generative tasks for safety and helpfulness. The use of public datasets and pre-trained models enhances the reproducibility and practical relevance of the results.  

## Weaknesses  
- The **illustrative examples** in the paper are conceptually clear but lack sufficient detail to fully validate the method's behavior. For instance, the synthetic dataset example in Figure 3 could benefit from a more explicit explanation of the training dynamics and how the final policy converges to the desired ranking.  
- The **description of hyperparameters and training specifics** is somewhat vague. While the paper mentions $\tau = 0.01$ and $\alpha = -0.5$, it does not provide a detailed justification for these choices or explore how sensitive the results are to different values. This limits the reproducibility of the experiments.  
- The **comparison to prior work** is strong, but the paper could better contextualize how Bi-FAPO differs from existing supervised alignment methods like DPO and IPO. For example, it is not entirely clear why the naive combination of datasets fails, and a more in-depth analysis of the limitations of these methods would strengthen the contribution.  
- The **results on pre-aligned models** (e.g., Zephyr-7b-beta) are promising, but the paper does not provide a detailed breakdown of how the method performs across different types of harmful prompts or safety categories. A more nuanced analysis of the generative tasks (e.g., toxicity, ethics) would help clarify the method's strengths and weaknesses in specific domains.  
- The **discussion of limitations** is brief and could be expanded. For example, the paper mentions the reliance on specific label formats but does not explore how this might affect the method's applicability to other alignment tasks or datasets with different annotation schemes.  

## Questions  
- How does the method handle cases where the safety and helpfulness labels are ambiguous or conflicting? The paper suggests a preference for safety, but it is unclear how the algorithm resolves situations where the safety label is low but the helpfulness label is high.  
- What is the **impact of the shift parameter $\alpha$** on the final model's behavior? The ablation study shows a slight improvement in generative tasks when $\alpha = -0.5$, but the paper does not explore a broader range of values or their effects on the trade-off between safety and helpfulness.  
- How does the **labeling function $g_I$** generalize to other types of preferences beyond safety and helpfulness? The paper claims the method is versatile, but it does not provide evidence or examples of this generalization.  
- What is the **computational cost of the safety classifier** used in the experiments (e.g., HarmBench-Llama2-13B-Chat)? While the paper emphasizes the reduction in human annotation, the use of a large pre-trained model for safety classification may still be resource-intensive.  
- How does the method perform when **trained on imbalanced datasets** (e.g., where safety data is much less frequent than helpfulness data)? The paper does not address this, but it is a critical concern for real-world applications.  

RATING: 8
