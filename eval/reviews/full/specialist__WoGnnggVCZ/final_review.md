## Summary  
The paper introduces GenDataAgent, a framework for on-the-fly synthetic data augmentation in computer vision. It dynamically generates synthetic data aligned with the target dataset distribution using a Stable Diffusion model, enhances diversity via Llama2-based caption perturbations, and filters out-of-distribution samples using a variance of gradients (VoG) strategy. The method is evaluated across multiple image classification datasets, showing improvements in generalization, fairness, and efficiency compared to static augmentation and prior on-the-fly approaches.  

## Strengths  
The paper makes a compelling case for the limitations of existing synthetic data augmentation methods and proposes a novel framework to address them. Key strengths include:  
- **Dynamic Feedback Mechanism**: GenDataAgent uses marginal samples and gradient variance to iteratively refine synthetic data generation, ensuring alignment with the target distribution and reducing outliers. This feedback loop is a significant departure from static augmentation methods.  
- **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The experiments span diverse datasets (e.g., ImageNet-100, Oxford-IIIT Pets, CUB-200-2011) and compare against multiple state-of-the-art baselines, including Real-Fake, ImageNetClone, and Internet Explorer. The results demonstrate consistent improvements in Top-1 accuracy and worst-case disparity, particularly in fine-grained classification tasks.  
- **Ablation Studies**: The paper thoroughly evaluates the contributions of its components (marginal sampling, Llama2 perturbation, VoG filtering) and their interactions. These ablations provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of each part of the framework.  
- **Efficiency and Practicality**: The time analysis in Figure 4 highlights the trade-off between synthetic data volume and performance, showing that GenDataAgent achieves strong results even with a small synthetic-to-real ratio (1:0.1). This suggests practical applicability in resource-constrained settings.  
- **Fairness Consideration**: The use of worst-case disparity as a fairness metric is a thoughtful addition, and the results indicate that GenDataAgent improves fairness compared to static augmentation methods.  

## Weaknesses  
While the paper presents a strong and well-motivated approach, there are notable weaknesses and concerns:  
- **Implementation Transparency**: The paper lacks sufficient detail on the exact implementation of the reproduced baselines (e.g., Real-Fake, ImageNetClone, CiP). Without clear descriptions of their prompt engineering, diffusion model settings, and filtering strategies, the comparison may not be fully fair or reproducible.  
- **Real-Only Baseline Clarity**: In the synthetic-only experiments, the real-only baseline is not clearly described in terms of training conditions (e.g., hyperparameters, number of epochs). This could bias the comparison in favor of real data and obscure the true performance of synthetic-only methods.  
- **Theoretical Justification**: The motivation for using marginal scores and gradient variance as feedback and filtering criteria is primarily empirical. A more formal or theoretical explanation of why these metrics are effective would strengthen the paper’s contribution.  
- **Qualitative Analysis**: While the paper includes t-SNE visualizations, it lacks a detailed qualitative comparison of the synthetic data generated by GenDataAgent and the baselines. Including examples of generated images and their impact on model training would improve interpretability.  
- **Statistical Rigor**: The results are presented in tabular form but lack statistical measures (e.g., confidence intervals, p-values) to assess the significance of the improvements. This makes it difficult to determine whether the gains are meaningful or due to random variation.  
- **Broader Applicability**: The paper focuses exclusively on image classification. While this is a valid starting point, the lack of exploration into other tasks (e.g., object detection, segmentation) limits the perceived generality of the approach.  

## Questions  
1. Could the authors provide a more detailed description of how the baselines (e.g., Real-Fake, ImageNetClone) were implemented or reproduced, including their prompt engineering and filtering strategies?  
2. How does the real-only baseline in the synthetic-only experiments compare in terms of training conditions (e.g., hyperparameters, number of epochs)? Is it trained under the same settings as the synthetic-only models?  
3. What is the theoretical basis for using marginal scores and gradient variance as feedback and filtering criteria? Could the authors provide a formal analysis or justification for these choices?  
4. Are there qualitative examples of synthetic data generated by GenDataAgent and the baselines? How do these examples differ in terms of diversity and relevance to the target dataset?  
5. Could the authors include statistical measures (e.g., confidence intervals) to assess the significance of the performance improvements reported in the experiments?  
6. How does GenDataAgent perform when applied to other tasks beyond image classification, such as object detection or segmentation?
