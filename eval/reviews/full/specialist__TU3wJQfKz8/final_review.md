## Summary  
This paper introduces a novel proof paradigm for analyzing the last-iterate convergence of smooth Regret Matching+ (RM+) variants in learning Nash equilibria (NE). The paradigm leverages the equivalence between RM+ and Online Mirror Descent (OMD) to recover key properties like monotonicity and weak Minty variation inequality (MVI), which are essential for convergence analysis. The authors apply this approach to two existing algorithms (Smooth Extra-gradient RM+ and Smooth Predictive RM+) and demonstrate their last-iterate convergence in monotone games. They also propose a new algorithm, Smooth Optimistic Gradient RM+ (SOGRM+), which achieves last-iterate convergence in games satisfying the weak MVI, a broader class of games. Experimental results on two-player zero-sum matrix games show that SOGRM+ outperforms other algorithms in terms of convergence speed and robustness to parameter choices.

## Strengths  
The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by introducing a novel and succinct proof paradigm that addresses a critical gap in the analysis of RM+ variants. The approach is grounded in the equivalence between RM+ and OMD, which is a well-established concept in the literature, and the use of the tangent residual to measure the distance to NE is both mathematically rigorous and insightful. The authors successfully demonstrate the applicability of their paradigm by proving last-iterate convergence for SExRM+ and SPRM+ under weaker assumptions than prior works, and they extend the analysis to SOGRM+ in the context of weak MVI, which is the weakest known condition for RM+ variants. This is a significant advancement, as it broadens the scope of games where RM+ can be theoretically guaranteed to converge.  

The experimental evaluation is well-structured and provides empirical validation of the theoretical claims. The comparison with both smooth and non-smooth RM+ variants, as well as traditional OMD-based algorithms, is appropriate and highlights the practical benefits of the proposed methods. The results show that SOGRM+ achieves faster convergence and is less sensitive to parameter changes, which is a compelling demonstration of its effectiveness. The paper also provides a clear and detailed comparison with previous works, emphasizing the generality and theoretical improvements of their approach.  

## Weaknesses  
While the paper makes a strong theoretical contribution, the experimental evaluation is limited to two-player zero-sum matrix games. This restricts the scope of the results and does not fully validate the generality of the proposed paradigm in broader game classes, such as convex-concave or non-zero-sum games. Expanding the experiments to these settings would strengthen the paper’s impact.  

The ablation studies are insufficient to fully understand the role of smoothing and the design choices in SOGRM+. For example, the paper does not explicitly quantify the benefits of smoothing in terms of convergence speed or stability, nor does it provide a detailed breakdown of how the inner product transformation contributes to the algorithm’s performance. A more thorough ablation would improve the paper’s depth.  

The paper lacks full implementation details and does not make the code publicly available, which limits reproducibility. While the duality gap is a valid proxy for the tangent residual, the paper does not report the tangent residual itself, which is central to the theoretical analysis. Including this metric would better align the experiments with the theory.  

The assumptions on weak MVI are not fully explored, and the implications of the bound on $\rho$ are not clearly explained. Providing intuition or examples of games where this condition is satisfied but monotonicity is not would help readers understand the practical relevance of the theoretical results.  

## Questions  
1. How does the proposed RM+–OMD equivalence hold in non-standard game settings, such as non-zero-sum or convex-concave games? A more detailed derivation or discussion of these cases would clarify the generality of the approach.  
2. What is the exact impact of the inner product transformation in SOGRM+ compared to the standard Optimistic Gradient algorithm? A more explicit comparison would help understand the algorithm’s design choices.  
3. Can the paper provide a concrete example of a game where the weak MVI condition is satisfied but monotonicity is not, and demonstrate the performance of SOGRM+ in such a setting?  
4. Why was the tangent residual not used as a primary metric in the experiments, despite being central to the theoretical analysis? What are the computational challenges in calculating it?  

RATING: 8
