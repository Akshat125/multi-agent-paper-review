## Summary  
The paper introduces a novel cooperative mean field game (MFG) model based on Chung-Lu (CL) graphs, which are particularly suited for sparse networks with finite first moment and infinite second moment. It addresses the limitations of existing MFG models, such as graphon MFGs and graphex-based MFGs, which are not well-suited for such sparse topologies. The authors propose a two systems approximation to manage the complexity of the limiting system, and they develop scalable learning algorithms for this framework. The paper includes experiments on both synthetic and real-world networks, showing that their approach outperforms existing methods in several cases. The contributions are framed as the introduction of CLCMFGs, a theoretical analysis, the two systems approximation, and empirical validation across multiple problems.

## Strengths  
The paper makes a meaningful contribution by addressing a significant gap in the MFG literature: the modeling of sparse networks with diverging degree variance. The use of Chung-Lu graphs is a natural and theoretically grounded choice, as they are known to capture realistic sparse structures and have convergence properties that align with the paper's analysis. The two systems approximation is a clever and practical solution to the intractability of the full limiting system, and the theoretical results (e.g., Theorem 1 and Proposition 1) provide a solid foundation for the proposed model. The experiments on real-world networks like Enron, Slashdot, and CAIDA add practical relevance, and the comparison with Lp graphon and graphex-based MFGs highlights the novelty of the approach. The paper also opens up promising directions for future research, such as extending the model to partial observability or bounded rationality settings.

## Weaknesses  
The paper lacks sufficient detail in several key areas that would strengthen its impact and reproducibility. The reward functions used in the experiments are not explicitly defined, making it difficult to assess the design choices and their alignment with the problem objectives. The learning algorithms (Algorithms 1 and 2) are described at a high level but lack pseudocode or implementation specifics, which hinders reproducibility. Additionally, the paper does not provide a dedicated ablation study on the threshold $ k^* $, a critical parameter in the two systems approximation. The experimental analysis is also limited in depth: there is no statistical significance testing, and the results are not quantitatively compared in terms of final objective values. The paper could also benefit from a more explicit discussion of the approximation error introduced by the two systems method and its trade-offs with computational complexity. Finally, the absence of a public code repository or dataset is a major concern for reproducibility and further research.

## Questions  
1. What is the exact form of the reward function $ r $ used in the SIS, SIR, Color, and Rumor problems, and how was it designed to reflect the cooperative nature of the tasks?  
2. How sensitive is the performance of the two systems approximation to the choice of $ k^* $, and what criteria were used to select the threshold in the experiments?  
3. What is the computational cost of the proposed learning algorithms compared to existing methods, and how does the approximation error scale with increasing $ k^* $?  
4. Could the authors provide a comparison of their method with non-cooperative MFG models on the same sparse networks to better understand the trade-offs between cooperation and competition?  
5. Are there plans to release the code and datasets used in the experiments to facilitate independent verification and further research?  

RATING: 7
