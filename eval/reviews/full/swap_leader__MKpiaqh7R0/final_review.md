## Summary  
The paper introduces a novel approach called Input Compensation (IC) to enhance the performance of pruned models. Unlike existing methods that operate in the parameter space by adjusting unpruned weights, IC operates in the input space by learning a compensation pool to construct input-dependent adjustments. The method leverages an attention mechanism to combine candidate compensations, enabling the pruned model to approximate the output of the dense model. Experiments on image classification, language modeling, and image generation tasks demonstrate that IC consistently improves the performance of pruned models when combined with state-of-the-art pruning techniques like Magnitude Pruning, Wanda, and SparseGPT. The paper claims IC is complementary to existing methods and offers minimal additional parameters, making it a practical solution for resource-constrained deployment.  

## Strengths  
The paper's main strengths lie in its innovative approach and strong empirical validation. The concept of input compensation is a fresh perspective in model pruning, addressing a gap in the literature where most methods focus on parameter-space adjustments. The methodology is well-motivated by theoretical insights from linear layer approximations and attention mechanisms, and the design of input-dependent compensation is clearly articulated. The experiments are comprehensive, covering diverse tasks (image classification, language modeling, image generation) and models (CLIP, LLaMA, DDPM), with results showing significant performance gains. For example, IC improves accuracy by 28% on ViT-B/32 for image classification and reduces perplexity by over 60% in language modeling. The ablation studies further validate the effectiveness of input-dependent compensation over input-independent variants and explore the sensitivity of key hyperparameters like rank $ r $. The minimal parameter overhead (e.g., 2.3M additional parameters) is also a practical advantage.  

## Weaknesses  
While the paper makes a compelling case for IC, there are several weaknesses and concerns. First, the experimental setup lacks sufficient detail in critical areas, such as the exact procedure for determining the pruning threshold $ \beta $ and the initialization/update rules for the compensation pool $ (\mathbf{K}, \mathbf{V}) $. This could hinder reproducibility. Second, the paper does not address the computational cost of training the compensation pool, which may be non-trivial for large models like LLaMA. Third, the comparison with input-dependent prompting methods is missing, despite the paper's claim that IC differs from such approaches. Including these would strengthen the novelty argument. Fourth, the statistical significance of the results is not discussed, leaving uncertainty about whether the improvements are robust. Finally, the paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of the encoder $ \mathcal{E}(\cdot) $'s role in the general IC framework, particularly in NLP tasks where input embeddings are modified.  

## Questions  
1. What is the computational overhead of training the compensation pool $ (\mathbf{K}, \mathbf{V}) $, especially for large models like LLaMA-3.1?  
2. How does the performance of IC compare to input-dependent prompting methods (e.g., conditional prompting) in tasks like image classification and language modeling?  
3. Are the improvements in performance (e.g., 28% on ViT-B/32) statistically significant, and what is the confidence interval for these results?  
4. Could the encoder $ \mathcal{E}(\cdot) $ in the general IC framework be replaced with alternative architectures (e.g., a learned projection layer) to improve performance further?
