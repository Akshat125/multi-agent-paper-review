## Summary
The paper introduces Activation Transport (AT), a general framework for steering activations in large generative models (LLMs and T2I models) using optimal transport (OT) theory. AT generalizes prior methods by modeling activation steering as a transport map between source and target distributions, enabling fine-grained control over model behavior with minimal computational overhead. The method is evaluated on tasks such as toxicity mitigation, concept induction, and truthfulness in LLMs, as well as style control and concept negation in T2I models. The results show that AT outperforms or matches existing methods in these tasks while maintaining model utility and interpretability through a bounded strength parameter $\lambda$.

## Strengths
The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by unifying various activation steering methods under the umbrella of optimal transport, offering a principled and interpretable approach to model control. The proposed method, \linear, is computationally efficient and modality-agnostic, successfully applied to both LLMs and T2I models without hyperparameter tuning. The experiments are comprehensive, covering a range of tasks and models, and the results are supported by both quantitative metrics (e.g., toxicity reduction, PPL, MMLU accuracy) and qualitative visualizations. The paper also introduces the concept of transport support to avoid out-of-distribution (OOD) activation issues, which is a novel and practical addition. The comparison with prior methods is thorough, and the ablation studies on pooling operators and layer choices provide insights into the robustness of the approach.

## Weaknesses
While the paper presents a compelling framework, there are some limitations. The method assumes univariate and linear transport maps, which may not capture complex, multimodal activation distributions. The paper does not provide sufficient detail on the computational resources used or the exact hyperparameters for training and evaluation, which could hinder reproducibility. Additionally, the experimental setup for dataset selection and prompt generation is not fully described, leaving some ambiguity about how the results were obtained. The paper could also benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the assumptions made and their potential impact on performance, as well as a broader comparison to state-of-the-art methods beyond those explicitly mentioned. Finally, the interpretability of the method, while claimed, is not sufficiently demonstrated with concrete examples or analysis.

## Questions
1. How does the performance of \linear change when applied to multimodal activation distributions, and what modifications would be needed to handle such cases?
2. Could the paper provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational overhead of \linear compared to other methods, especially in terms of memory usage and inference time?
3. What are the specific hyperparameters used in the experiments, and how were they chosen? Were any hyperparameter tuning steps performed, and if so, how were they structured?
4. How does the method perform on more complex or diverse datasets beyond the ones used in the experiments, and what are the potential failure modes in such scenarios?
5. Can the paper provide a more detailed comparison to other state-of-the-art activation steering methods that were not included in the current evaluation?
