## Summary
The paper "Agree to Disagree: Demystifying Homogeneous Deep Ensembles through Distributional Equivalence" presents a novel theoretical explanation for the effectiveness of deep ensembles. It challenges the traditional view that the Jensen gap is the primary reason for performance improvement and instead introduces the concept of distributional equivalence. The authors argue that the predictions of homogeneous models, despite being point-wise different, form an identical distribution over the data space. This property, combined with the global diversity of predictions, is shown to be the key to the success of deep ensembles. The paper provides rigorous theoretical derivations and extensive empirical validations using large ensembles (M=100) across multiple datasets and model architectures.

## Strengths
- The paper introduces a **novel theoretical perspective** on deep ensembles, focusing on the **distributional equivalence property**. This is a significant departure from the traditional Jensen gap explanation and provides a more accurate and comprehensive understanding of the mechanism behind ensemble performance.
- The **theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-justified**, with clear derivations and appropriate assumptions. The authors provide **closed-form expressions** for the expected loss of ensembles under both Brier score and NLL, which is a first in the literature.
- The **empirical validation is extensive and well-motivated**, using a large number of ensemble members (M=100) across multiple datasets and model architectures. This ensures that the findings are **generalizable** and not limited to specific datasets or models.
- The paper **distinguishes between the mechanisms of ensembling and model scaling**, a long-standing debate in the field. This distinction is important for understanding the **trade-offs in model design and resource allocation**.
- The **practical contribution** of the paper is notable: it introduces a **cost-effective estimation method** for predicting the performance of deep ensembles using only two models. This is a powerful tool for practitioners who want to **forecast the benefits of ensembling** without incurring the full computational cost.

## Weaknesses
- The **origin of the distributional equivalence property** is not fully explained. While the authors suggest it may be due to **distribution shift** or other properties of the training process, this is left as a future direction. A more detailed **intuition or hypothesis** for why this property holds would strengthen the paper.
- The **assumption of complete neural collapse** is a strong one, and the paper does not provide a **detailed discussion of its limitations** or how it affects the generalizability of the results. While the authors note that real-world models exhibit a tendency to collapse, a more explicit **analysis of the assumptions** would improve the paper's robustness.
- The **empirical validation of the global gap (GG)** is not as direct as it could be. The paper provides **empirical validation of the Brier score and NLL**, but the **global gap itself** is not clearly demonstrated or discussed in detail.
- The **notational and terminological clarity** could be improved. There are a few **notational inconsistencies** and **unclear definitions** in the paper (e.g., the use of $\hat{\mathcal{F}}$ and $\mathcal{F}$, the meaning of $\rho$, and the exact form of the Brier score and NLL in the theorems). These should be **clarified in the main text** to avoid confusion for readers unfamiliar with the notation.
- The **interpretation of the Wasserstein distance and Kolmogorov-Smirnov test** is somewhat brief. A more detailed explanation of **what the Wasserstein distances and p-values indicate** in the context of model prediction distributions would improve the clarity of the empirical validation.
- The paper **does not fully explore the implications of the distributional equivalence property for uncertainty quantification**, despite mentioning that deep ensembles are known for their **superior UQ**. A more in-depth analysis of how the proposed framework affects **uncertainty estimation** would broaden the impact of the work.
- The **comparison with majority vote ensembles** is limited. While the paper briefly mentions this variant, it does not provide a **detailed comparison** of the theoretical and empirical performance of majority vote vs. averaging. This could be an interesting **avenue for future work**.

## Questions
- What is the **exact origin of the distributional equivalence property** in deep ensembles? Is it a result of the **training process**, **model architecture**, or **data distribution**?
- How **robust is the distributional equivalence property** to changes in the **training regime** (e.g., different optimizers, learning rates, or data augmentation strategies)? Could one design a training procedure that **breaks the distributional equivalence** and observe a degradation in ensemble performance?
- What is the **practical significance of the global gap (GG)** in real-world applications? How does it **compare to other ensemble evaluation metrics** in terms of **predictive power and interpretability**?
- How does the **proposed estimation scheme using only two models** perform in **regression tasks**? Is the **distributional equivalence property** also relevant in **non-classification settings**?
- What are the **limitations of the complete neural collapse assumption** in the theoretical derivations? How does this assumption **affect the generalizability** of the results to **real-world models** that may not fully collapse?
- How does the **distributional equivalence property** affect the **uncertainty quantification** of deep ensembles? Could the **proposed framework** be used to **improve UQ** in other ensemble methods?

RATING: 8
