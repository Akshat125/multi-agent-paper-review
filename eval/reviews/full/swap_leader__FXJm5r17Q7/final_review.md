## Summary  
The paper introduces the Decision Importance Transformer (DIT), a supervised pretraining framework for in-context reinforcement learning (ICRL) that leverages suboptimal historical data. Unlike prior methods such as DPT and AD, which require optimal action labels or complete learning histories, DIT uses a weighted maximum likelihood estimation loss guided by an in-context advantage function estimator to improve over suboptimal policies. The method is evaluated on bandit and MDP problems, including challenging environments like Dark Room, Miniworld, MetaWorld, and Half-Cheetah, where it demonstrates competitive or superior performance compared to existing approaches. The paper also discusses the theoretical basis for DIT and its limitations, such as the need for behavioral policies with reasonable rewards.

## Strengths  
- **Novelty in Data Utilization**: DIT addresses a critical limitation in ICRL by enabling pretraining with suboptimal data, which is more practical to collect in real-world settings. This is a significant departure from prior work that relies on optimal or near-optimal trajectories.  
- **Strong Empirical Results**: The experiments show that DIT matches or outperforms state-of-the-art methods like DPT and AD in both online and offline settings. For example, in bandit problems, DIT achieves performance comparable to theoretically optimal algorithms like Thompson Sampling and UCB, even without access to optimal labels during pretraining.  
- **Theoretical Justification**: The paper provides a clear theoretical motivation for the weighted pretraining objective, linking it to policy improvement guarantees. The use of advantage functions to reweight suboptimal data is well-justified and aligns with established RL principles.  
- **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The experiments span a diverse set of environments, including both discrete (bandits) and continuous (MetaWorld, Half-Cheetah) control tasks. The comparison with multiple baselines (SAC, AD, BC, PDT) ensures a robust validation of the method.  
- **Ablation Studies**: The ablation on the weighted pretraining framework effectively demonstrates its importance, as the unweighted version (BC) performs significantly worse. The comparison with a restricted DPT variant further highlights DIT’s strengths in leveraging in-trajectory data.

## Weaknesses  
- **Limited Theoretical Depth**: While the paper mentions policy improvement guarantees, it lacks rigorous proofs or deeper theoretical analysis to substantiate these claims. This weakens the confidence in the method’s robustness and generalizability.  
- **Ambiguity in "Reasonable Rewards"**: The paper states that DIT requires behavioral policies to have "reasonable rewards," but it does not define what this entails or how it might affect performance in practice. This could limit the method’s applicability to real-world scenarios with highly suboptimal data.  
- **Implementation and Hyperparameter Details**: The paper provides minimal details on hyperparameters (e.g., learning rates, optimizer settings) and implementation specifics (e.g., how the advantage estimator is trained). This hinders reproducibility and makes it difficult to assess the method’s sensitivity to these choices.  
- **Lack of Generalization to Complex Domains**: The experiments focus on relatively well-structured environments. The paper does not explore how DIT might perform in more complex or high-dimensional settings, such as real-world robotics or partially observable tasks.  
- **Ethical Discussion is Superficial**: The ethics statement is brief and does not engage deeply with potential risks, such as the use of historical data from non-expert users in safety-critical applications. A more thorough discussion would strengthen the paper’s impact.

## Questions  
1. The paper claims DIT guarantees policy improvement over suboptimal behavior policies. Could the authors provide a formal proof or a more detailed theoretical analysis to support this assertion?  
2. How does the performance of DIT change when the pretraining data contains highly suboptimal or random trajectories (e.g., with average rewards significantly lower than 30% of optimal)? The paper mentions this is unlikely, but the threshold is not clearly defined.  
3. The paper uses GPT-2 due to computational constraints. What is the expected performance gain with larger transformer models, and have the authors explored this in preliminary experiments?  
4. The advantage estimator is described as "interpolating across tasks," but the paper does not explain how this interpolation is achieved. Could the authors provide more details on the architecture and training of the advantage estimator?  
5. The reproducibility statement mentions hyperparameters are provided, but the main text lacks specific values. Could the authors include a table summarizing all hyperparameters used in the experiments for clarity?  

RATING: 8
