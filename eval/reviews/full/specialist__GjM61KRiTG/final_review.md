## Summary
The paper introduces Bi-Factorial Preference Optimization (Bi-FAPO), a supervised learning framework that re-parameterizes a multi-objective RLHF objective for balancing safety and helpfulness in large language models (LLMs). The method uses a labeling function to capture global preference rankings, enabling the model to optimize both objectives simultaneously. The authors present a benchmark with discriminative and generative tasks to evaluate helpfulness and harmlessness, and they demonstrate that Bi-FAPO outperforms existing approaches in both metrics. The method is computationally efficient, requiring less than 10% of the resources of traditional RLHF methods and eliminating the need for human prompting and annotation. The paper also provides theoretical equivalence between the proposed supervised optimization and multi-objective RLHF, and includes ablation studies to validate key components of the method.

## Strengths
The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of LLM alignment by proposing a novel supervised learning framework that effectively balances safety and helpfulness. The method is well-motivated, addressing a critical challenge in the field: the inherent tension between these two objectives. The theoretical equivalence between the proposed supervised optimization and multi-objective RLHF is a strong foundation for the method, and the derivation of the label function $g_I$ is mathematically sound. The experiments are comprehensive, including both helpfulness and harmlessness evaluation, and the results demonstrate that Bi-FAPO outperforms existing methods in terms of safety and helpfulness. The method is also computationally efficient, which is a major advantage over traditional RLHF approaches that require extensive human annotation and red teaming. The red teaming experiment further validates the method's ability to improve safety without requiring additional human intervention, and the ablation study provides insights into the effectiveness of the shift parameter $\alpha$ and buffered training.

## Weaknesses
While the paper presents a promising method, there are several areas where the experimental design and methodology could be improved. The training procedure lacks sufficient detail, particularly regarding the optimizer, learning rate schedule, and evaluation protocol. This limits the reproducibility of the results and makes it difficult to understand the training dynamics. The red teaming data preparation is not sufficiently detailed, and the paper should clarify how the 9K harmful prompts were selected and how the 1.5K red teaming data was generated and filtered. The ablation study is a good start but could be more comprehensive, including a sensitivity analysis of the method to different hyperparameters and a more detailed analysis of failure cases. The paper also does not provide a clear definition of what it means by "helpful" and "harmless" in the context of the evaluation, which could lead to ambiguity in interpreting the results. Additionally, the paper should discuss the limitations of using public datasets and pre-trained classifiers for safety and helpfulness, as these may not fully capture the nuances of human preferences.

## Questions
1. How were the specific layers (second MLP layers in each transformer block) chosen for unfreezing during training, and was this decision tested against other layer selection strategies?
2. What is the exact optimizer and learning rate schedule used in the training process, and how were these hyperparameters determined?
3. How do the safety labels in the public datasets compare to human annotations, and are there known biases or limitations in the classifiers used for evaluation?
4. What is the distribution of safety and helpfulness in the red teaming dataset, and how were the 9K harmful prompts selected from the PKU-SafeRLHF dataset?
5. How sensitive is the method to the choice of $\tau$, and what is the impact of varying $B_1$ and $B_3$ in the labeling function $g_I$?
6. Could the paper provide more qualitative examples to illustrate the method's behavior on prompts that are both harmful and unhelpful, or on prompts that are safe but unhelpful?
