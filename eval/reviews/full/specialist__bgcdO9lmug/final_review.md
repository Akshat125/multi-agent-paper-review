## Summary  
The paper introduces RePrompt, a novel method for optimizing prompts for large language model (LLM) agents using a gradient descent-like approach. It leverages chat history and reflections from LLM-agent interactions to iteratively refine prompts without requiring a final solution checker, which is often expensive or unavailable in real-world applications. The method is tested on two reasoning tasks: PDDL generation and travel planning. In PDDL generation, RePrompt improves the success rate of action definitions and reduces the need for expert annotations. In travel planning, it enhances the final pass rate by addressing macro commonsense constraints, though it struggles with hallucination errors. The paper also includes an ablation study on training sample size and epochs, showing that moderate sample sizes yield the best results. Overall, RePrompt is positioned as a generalizable framework for improving LLM agents through prompt engineering, with a focus on step-by-step instruction refinement.

## Strengths  
The paper makes a clear and novel contribution by addressing a critical challenge in LLM agents: the high cost of final evaluation in prompt engineering. RePrompt's use of intermediate feedback and reflections is a practical and innovative approach, particularly in domains like travel planning where final evaluation is difficult. The experiments on PDDL generation and travel planning demonstrate tangible improvements in success rates and pass rates, respectively, and the method generalizes to other domains without requiring additional fine-tuning of the LLMs themselves. The ablation study provides useful insights into the trade-offs between training sample size and the number of epochs, aligning with the gradient descent analogy. Additionally, the paper identifies and addresses practical issues like incomplete prompt generation and incorrect format changes, showing awareness of real-world implementation challenges.

## Weaknesses  
The paper's methodology lacks sufficient detail to fully reproduce the experiments. For instance, the implementation specifics of the summarizer and prompt optimizer are not clearly defined, and the paper does not provide pseudocode or a step-by-step algorithm for RePrompt. The experimental design also has limitations: the Travel Planner task uses only 10 training samples, which is insufficient to validate the method's robustness. Furthermore, the paper does not compare RePrompt with other prompt engineering techniques like Chain-of-Thought (CoT) or Tree-of-Thought in the PDDL generation task, making it unclear whether the improvements are due to the method itself or simply the addition of more instructions. The error analysis is also limited, particularly in addressing hallucination errors, which are a known issue in LLM agents. The paper could have provided a more rigorous evaluation of the post-processing steps used to handle incomplete or incorrect prompts, and a cost analysis would have strengthened the claim that RePrompt reduces the need for expensive feedback.

## Questions  
1. How are the summarizer and prompt optimizer implemented in RePrompt? Are they using the same LLM (e.g., GPT-4) or different models, and what specific prompts or instructions are given to them?  
2. Why is one epoch sufficient for the PDDL generation task, and how is convergence determined? Is there a stopping criterion based on performance improvement, or is it arbitrary?  
3. What is the exact baseline prompt used in the PDDL generation task, and how does RePrompt's improvement compare to other prompt engineering techniques like CoT or Tree-of-Thought?  
4. How often does the post-processing step (e.g., using an additional LLM to fill in templates) fail, and how does this affect the final performance in the Travel Planner task?  
5. Could the summarizer be modified to include hallucination detection, and if so, how might this impact the results?  

RATING: 7
