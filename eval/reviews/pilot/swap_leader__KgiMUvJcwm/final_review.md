## Summary  
The paper introduces **IgSeek**, a novel structure-retrieval framework for antibody design, specifically targeting the challenge of hallucinations in sequence inference for Complementarity-Determining Regions (CDRs). IgSeek leverages a **multi-channel equivariant graph neural network (MEGNN)** to generate geometric representations of CDR backbone structures, enabling efficient retrieval of structurally similar CDRs from a natural antibody database. By aligning sequences of these retrieved CDRs and using structurally conserved motifs, IgSeek improves sequence recovery accuracy. The method is evaluated on multiple datasets, including SAbDab, STCRDab, and OAS-H3, and is shown to outperform state-of-the-art models in both accuracy and inference speed. The paper also highlights the potential of retrieval-based strategies in mitigating hallucinations and advancing therapeutic protein design.

## Strengths  
- **Novelty and Framework Design**: IgSeek presents a fresh perspective by shifting from generative models to a retrieval-based approach for CDR sequence design. This is particularly impactful given the high hallucination rates in existing methods like ProteinMPNN and ESM-IF1. The use of MEGNN to encode structural information in a way that is invariant to E(3) transformations is a strong technical contribution.  
- **Strong Empirical Results**: The experiments demonstrate that IgSeek achieves **superior sequence recovery rates** (e.g., outperforming competitors by at least 2.9% on light chain CDRs and 30% on TCR CDRs) and a **2.6x speed-up** in structure retrieval compared to FoldSeek. These results are supported by comparisons on both solved and predicted structures, as well as a case study on a specific CDR loop (PDB 8W8R CDR-L1), which further validates the method's effectiveness.  
- **Generalization and Efficiency**: The model generalizes well to unseen data (e.g., STCRDab dataset) and is efficient enough for high-throughput applications. The visualization of CDR embeddings using t-SNE shows that the model captures meaningful structural clusters, reinforcing the quality of its representations.  
- **Theoretical Justification**: The paper provides a clear theoretical foundation for the MEGNN encoder, including a theorem that establishes its E(3) equivariance and invariance properties. This adds credibility to the method's design and generalization capabilities.  

## Weaknesses  
- **Lack of Code and Data Availability**: The paper does not mention the availability of code or datasets, which is a critical issue for reproducibility. Without access to the implementation, it is difficult to verify the results or build upon the work.  
- **Insufficient Methodological Details**: While the MEGNN architecture is described, key components such as the exact number of layers, hidden dimensions, and activation functions are omitted. The paper also lacks a detailed explanation of the self-supervised training objective and the rationale for using RMSD as a secondary sorting metric in the IgSeek+Kabsch variant.  
- **Limited Ablation Studies**: The ablation studies are minimal. For example, the impact of hyperparameters like the number of nearest neighbors (K) or the perturbation magnitude (σ) is not thoroughly explored. A more detailed comparison between IgSeek and IgSeek+Kabsch would strengthen the paper's claims.  
- **Ambiguity in Dataset Construction**: The criteria for selecting CDR pairs in the training and test sets are not clearly explained. For instance, the process of sampling from SAbDab and the rationale for excluding certain CDRs in the evaluation are not detailed, which could raise concerns about the robustness of the results.  
- **Overemphasis on Speed**: While the speed-up compared to FoldSeek is notable, the paper does not address whether this efficiency gain is due to the MEGNN architecture or the retrieval strategy. A more nuanced discussion of the trade-offs between speed and accuracy would improve the paper's depth.  

## Questions  
- The paper claims that IgSeek+Kabsch outperforms all baselines. Could the authors provide a detailed ablation study comparing the performance of IgSeek and IgSeek+Kabsch across all CDR types to clarify the contribution of the RMSD-based secondary sorting?  
- The MEGNN encoder is described as a multi-channel equivariant graph neural network, but the exact architecture (e.g., number of layers, hidden dimensions, and activation functions) is not specified. Could the authors provide these details to better understand the model's design and capacity?  
- The paper mentions that AntiFold and AbMPNN show performance degradation on the SAbDab-2024 dataset compared to prior work. Could the authors elaborate on the specific structural or sequence characteristics of the SAbDab-2024 dataset that might explain this discrepancy?  
- The self-supervised training objective is based on predicting RMSD between CDR pairs. Why was RMSD chosen over other structural similarity metrics like TM-score or GDT-TA? Could the authors justify this choice and discuss its implications for the model's performance?  
- The paper does not provide a comparison of IgSeek's performance with other retrieval-based methods in terms of structural alignment quality. Could the authors include a comparison with methods like AlphaFind or other structure retrieval tools to contextualize their results?  

RATING: 8
