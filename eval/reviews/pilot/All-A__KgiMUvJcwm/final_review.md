## Summary  
The paper introduces **IgSeek**, a novel framework for antibody design that addresses the problem of hallucinations in sequence inference for Complementarity-Determining Regions (CDRs). Unlike traditional generative models, which often produce non-functional sequences due to inaccuracies in folding, IgSeek leverages a **structure-retrieval approach** to infer CDR sequences by finding structurally similar templates in a database. The method employs a **multi-channel equivariant graph neural network (MEGNN)** to generate geometric representations of CDR backbone structures, ensuring invariance to 3D transformations. The paper demonstrates that IgSeek outperforms state-of-the-art methods in both **sequence recovery accuracy** and **inference speed**, with notable improvements on CDR-H3 loops, which are particularly challenging due to their high variability. The approach is validated on multiple datasets, including solved and predicted antibody structures, and shows strong generalization to T-cell receptor (TCR) sequences.  

## Strengths  
- **Novelty of the Retrieval-Based Framework**: IgSeek introduces a fundamentally different approach to antibody design by using structure retrieval instead of sequence generation. This is a significant departure from existing methods like ProteinMPNN and AntiFold, which rely on generative models and are prone to hallucinations. The retrieval-based strategy is well-motivated by the empirical observation that structurally similar CDRs often share conserved sequence motifs, and the paper effectively demonstrates how this can be leveraged to improve inference accuracy.  
- **Strong Performance on Key Metrics**: The experiments show that IgSeek achieves a **2.6x speed-up** in structure retrieval compared to FoldSeek, while maintaining or improving accuracy. It also outperforms other methods in **sequence recovery**, with a **2.9% improvement** on light chain CDR loops and **30% improvement** on TCR datasets. The performance on CDR-H3 loops is particularly impressive, as these are known to be the most challenging due to their structural diversity.  
- **Efficiency and Practical Relevance**: The efficiency of IgSeek is a major strength, especially for high-throughput applications. The **20x speed-up** in inference time compared to baseline methods is a compelling result that could make the model more practical for real-world antibody design workflows.  
- **Theoretical Justification and Visualization**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the MEGNN architecture, including the **E(3) equivariance** property, which is crucial for modeling 3D structures. The T-SNE visualization of CDR embeddings (Fig. 5) effectively demonstrates the model's ability to cluster structurally similar CDRs, supporting the claim that the learned representations are meaningful.  
- **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The paper evaluates IgSeek on a variety of datasets (SAbDab, STCRDab, OAS-H3) and compares it to multiple state-of-the-art models (FoldSeek, ProteinMPNN, ESM-IF1, AbMPNN, AntiFold). This broad evaluation strengthens the claim that IgSeek is a robust and generalizable method.  

## Weaknesses  
- **Limited Ablation Studies**: While the paper includes a comparison between IgSeek and IgSeek+Kabsch (which uses RMSD as a secondary sorting metric), the ablation studies are not comprehensive. The impact of key design choices, such as the multi-channel architecture, the equivariance property, and the READOUT function, is not thoroughly analyzed. A more detailed ablation would help readers understand which components are most critical to the model's performance.  
- **Ambiguity in Baseline Comparisons**: The comparison with AntiFold and AbMPNN is somewhat problematic, as these models use both CDR and framework region information, while IgSeek is only given the CDR structure. The paper should clarify whether the comparison is fair or if the other models are being evaluated under a different setting. If the comparison is not directly comparable, the results may not fully reflect the superiority of IgSeek.  
- **Insufficient Discussion of Hallucinations**: The paper mentions that hallucinations are a major issue in protein inverse folding, but it does not provide a direct evaluation of how much IgSeek reduces them. For example, the paper could report the number of non-functional sequences generated by each method or compare the structural deviation of the predicted sequences using a structure prediction tool like AlphaFold. This would provide a more concrete assessment of the model's ability to avoid hallucinations.  
- **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing**: The paper reports average AAR values but does not provide statistical significance testing (e.g., p-values, confidence intervals) to determine whether the improvements are statistically significant. This is a standard practice in machine learning and would strengthen the paper's claims.  
- **Missing Implementation and Hyperparameter Details**: The paper does not provide sufficient details on the implementation of the MEGNN model, such as the number of layers, hidden dimensions, and MLP architectures. Additionally, the hyperparameters used in training and inference (e.g., learning rate, batch size, optimizer) are not specified, which hinders reproducibility.  
- **No Mention of Code or Data Availability**: The paper does not state whether the code or data used in the experiments are publicly available. This is a critical point for reproducibility and for allowing others to build upon the work.  
- **Ambiguous Self-Supervised Learning Task**: The self-supervised learning task used to train the MEGNN is not clearly defined. The paper should specify the threshold for TM-Score used to define "similar" CDR pairs and the exact method for computing RMSD. These details are important for understanding the training process and for replicating the model.  
- **Vague Figure Captions**: The captions for Figures 2, 3, and 4 are not detailed enough to stand on their own. For example, the caption for Figure 2 does not clearly explain what the AUROC metric represents in the context of CDR retrieval. A more detailed explanation of the metrics and methods used in the figures would improve clarity.  
- **No Summary of Key Results**: The paper presents a large number of experimental results but does not provide a clear summary of the main findings. A table or a paragraph summarizing the AAR improvements and speed-up factors would help readers quickly grasp the significance of the work.  
- **Limited Discussion of Practical Implications**: While the paper highlights the efficiency of IgSeek, it does not provide a detailed discussion of how the model can be integrated into existing antibody design pipelines or what advantages it offers over current methods in practical applications. This would help readers understand the real-world impact of the work.  

## Questions  
- **What is the exact threshold for TM-Score used to define "similar" CDR pairs in the self-supervised learning task?** This is important for understanding how the model is trained and for replicating the process.  
- **How does the model handle CDRs of different lengths?** The paper mentions that the K-NN search is performed on CDRs of equal length, but it does not explain how the model deals with CDRs of varying lengths or whether length is a critical factor in the retrieval process.  
- **What is the exact distance metric used in the K-NN search?** The paper states that the top-10 nearest neighbors are retrieved, but it does not specify whether this is based on Euclidean distance, cosine similarity, or another metric. This is a key detail for understanding the retrieval mechanism.  
- **How does the model ensure that the retrieved sequences are functionally relevant?** The paper focuses on structural similarity, but it does not discuss how the model ensures that the retrieved sequences are functionally compatible with the target antigen. This is a critical concern for practical applications in antibody design.  
- **What is the computational cost of training the MEGNN model?** The paper does not provide a comparison of the computational resources required for training and inference, which is important for assessing the scalability of the method.  
- **How does the model perform on CDRs with rare or novel structures?** The paper does not address the limitations of the retrieval-based approach when dealing with CDRs that do not have close structural matches in the database. This is an important consideration for the generalizability of the method.  
- **What is the exact procedure for aligning the retrieved sequences?** The paper mentions that the retrieved sequences are aligned, but it does not specify the alignment method or the criteria used to select the best alignment. This is a key detail for understanding the sequence generation process.  
- **How does the model handle the high variability of CDR-H3 loops?** While the results show that IgSeek performs well on CDR-H3, the paper does not provide a detailed discussion of the architectural choices that enable this performance. This would help readers understand the model's strengths and limitations.
