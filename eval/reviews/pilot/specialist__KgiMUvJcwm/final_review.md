## Summary  
The paper introduces **IgSeek**, a novel structure-retrieval framework for antibody design that addresses the challenge of hallucinations in protein inverse folding. By leveraging a **multi-channel equivariant graph neural network (MEGNN)**, IgSeek generates geometric representations of CDR backbone structures and retrieves structurally similar CDRs from a database to infer sequences. The method is evaluated on both solved and predicted antibody structures, demonstrating superior performance in **sequence recovery accuracy** and **inference speed** compared to state-of-the-art models like FoldSeek, ProteinMPNN, and AntiFold. The paper also highlights generalization to T-cell receptor (TCR) CDRs and provides visualizations of the CDR embeddings to validate the model's ability to capture structural relationships.

## Strengths  
- **Novelty of the Retrieval-Based Approach**: IgSeek introduces a retrieval-based strategy for CDR sequence inference, which is distinct from traditional generative models. This approach reduces hallucinations by using structurally similar templates, a concept inspired by retrieval-augmented methods in other domains.  
- **Strong Performance on Key Metrics**: The experiments show that IgSeek outperforms existing methods in **average amino acid recovery (AAR)** by at least 2.9% for light chain CDRs and achieves comparable results for heavy chains. The **IgSeek⁺Kabsch variant** further improves performance, particularly on the hypervariable CDR-H3 loops.  
- **Efficiency and Scalability**: IgSeek demonstrates a **20x speed-up** in inference compared to baseline methods, which is critical for high-throughput applications in therapeutic protein design.  
- **Generalization to TCRs**: The model generalizes well to T-cell receptor CDRs without additional training, suggesting broader applicability beyond antibodies.  
- **Clear Methodological Contributions**: The paper provides a detailed description of the MEGNN architecture, including its **equivariance properties** and **self-supervised training objective**, which are theoretically sound and contribute to the field of geometric deep learning.  

## Weaknesses  
- **Limited Ablation Studies**: The ablation analysis is minimal, with only a comparison between the base IgSeek and the IgSeek⁺Kabsch variant. Key components such as the **number of MEGNN layers**, **READOUT function**, and **K-NN retrieval parameters** are not systematically tested, leaving the source of performance gains unclear.  
- **Insufficient Evaluation of Functional Outcomes**: While AAR is a useful metric, the paper lacks **functional validation** of the generated sequences (e.g., binding affinity or solubility). This limits the ability to assess whether the improved sequence recovery translates to biologically viable antibodies.  
- **Ambiguity in Baseline Comparisons**: The paper does not clarify whether baseline models (e.g., AbMPNN, AntiFold) were used with their **default configurations** or adapted for the CDR-specific task. This raises concerns about the **fairness of the comparison**, as some models may have an inherent advantage due to their design.  
- **Lack of Robustness Testing**: The model's performance is not evaluated under **noisy or incomplete input structures**, which are common in real-world scenarios. Additionally, the **impact of CDR length variation** on retrieval and sequence generation is not addressed.  
- **Missing Implementation Details**: The paper omits critical details such as the **distance metric used in K-NN**, the **ensemble strategy**, and the **sampling temperature** in Bernoulli sampling. These omissions hinder reproducibility and make it difficult to assess the method's practical utility.  

## Questions  
1. How does the performance of IgSeek degrade when the input CDR structure is noisy or incomplete, and what strategies are proposed to mitigate this?  
2. What is the functional viability of the sequences generated by IgSeek? Have the authors tested binding affinity or solubility of the predicted sequences in silico or in vitro?  
3. Could the retrieval-based approach be extended to design CDRs with specific antigen-binding properties, and how would the database be curated for such a task?  
4. What are the exact hyperparameters and configurations used for the baseline models in the comparison? Were they fine-tuned for the CDR-specific task?  
5. How does the choice of the READOUT function (e.g., mean pooling vs. attention-based aggregation) affect the model's performance, and was this tested in ablation studies?
