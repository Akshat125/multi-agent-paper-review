## Summary  
The paper introduces HOPE, a novel parameterization scheme for linear time-invariant (LTI) systems in state-space models (SSMs), grounded in Hankel operator theory. The authors identify that traditional SSMs, such as S4 and S4D, suffer from poor initialization and training stability due to the scarcity of high-rank LTI systems in their parameter space and the sensitivity of their system matrices to perturbations. HOPE addresses these issues by parameterizing LTI systems using Markov parameters of a Hankel matrix, which leads to improved numerical stability, reduced parameter count, and non-decaying memory within a fixed time window. The method is validated on the sCIFAR-10 task and Long-Range Arena (LRA) benchmarks, where it outperforms or matches state-of-the-art SSMs.

## Strengths  
The paper makes a compelling case for the importance of Hankel singular values in understanding and improving SSMs. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-structured, with clear proofs that support the claims of high numerical rank and stability under perturbations. The proposed HOPE parameterization is both novel and practical, reducing the number of parameters by a factor of 1/3 compared to S4D while maintaining or improving performance. The experiments are well-designed, with ablation studies on initialization and training stability that directly tie back to the theoretical insights. The demonstration of non-decaying memory on the noise-padded sCIFAR-10 task is particularly strong, as it empirically validates a key theoretical contribution. The comparison to related work is thorough, and the authors clearly position their method as a principled alternative to ad-hoc initialization and reparameterization techniques.

## Weaknesses  
The paper could improve in several areas. First, the reproducibility of the experiments is limited due to insufficient details on hyperparameters, training procedures, and the exact configurations used for the sCIFAR-10 and LRA tasks. The lack of publicly available code or data hinders the ability of others to reproduce the results. Second, while the theoretical analysis is sound, the connection between the Hankel singular values and the practical performance of SSMs is not fully explained. A more detailed discussion of how the singular values directly influence model behavior would strengthen the paper. Third, the figures and tables, while generally clear, could benefit from more descriptive captions and annotations to improve accessibility for non-experts. Finally, the paper does not explore the limitations of HOPE-SSMs in detail, such as potential trade-offs in computational efficiency or performance on tasks with very short-range dependencies.

## Questions  
1. Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how the Hankel singular values directly influence the model's ability to capture long-range dependencies?  
2. What are the specific hyperparameters used for training the HOPE-SSM on the LRA tasks, and how do they compare to those used for the S4D baseline?  
3. Is the non-decaying memory property of HOPE-SSMs preserved when the sampling period $\Delta t$ is tuned for different sequence lengths, and how does this affect the model's performance?  
4. How does the proposed method scale with larger state dimensions $n$, and are there any observed limitations in such cases?  
5. Could the authors clarify the computational overhead of the nonuniform sampling approach compared to traditional methods like S4D, especially for varying sequence lengths?

RATING: 8
