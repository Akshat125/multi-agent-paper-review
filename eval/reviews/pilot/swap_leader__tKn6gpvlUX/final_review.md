## Summary
The paper introduces scDCA, a method for efficiently fine-tuning single-cell foundation models (FMs) to predict transcriptional responses to novel molecular perturbations. The approach uses a drug-conditional adapter that allows training less than 1% of the original model's parameters, enabling molecular conditioning while preserving pre-trained biological representations. The method is evaluated on the sciplex3 dataset across four generalization tasks: unseen drugs, unseen drug-cell-line combinations, and both few-shot and zero-shot generalization to new cell lines. scDCA demonstrates state-of-the-art performance, particularly excelling in zero-shot settings where it outperforms existing baselines. The paper highlights the potential of scDCA for drug discovery and cellular modeling, while acknowledging limitations such as its reliance on transformer-based FMs and the need for control gene expression data.

## Strengths
The paper makes several notable contributions and strengths:
- **Novelty in Adapter Design**: The drug-conditional adapter is a novel approach to parameter-efficient fine-tuning, specifically tailored for integrating molecular information into pre-trained single-cell FMs. This design allows the model to dynamically adjust its parameters using molecule embeddings in a lower-dimensional space, significantly reducing the number of trainable parameters.
- **Robust Generalization**: The method is evaluated on a challenging zero-shot generalization task, where it must predict cellular responses for entirely unseen cell lines. This is a significant advancement over prior work, which primarily focused on seen cell lines and known drugs.
- **State-of-the-Art Performance**: scDCA consistently outperforms existing baselines across all tasks, with the most notable improvements in the zero-shot and few-shot settings. The results are supported by statistical analysis, including standard error reporting, which strengthens the validity of the claims.
- **Efficiency and Practicality**: The parameter-efficient nature of scDCA makes it computationally feasible to apply to large pre-trained models with limited data, which is a critical advantage in the context of data-scarce biological tasks.
- **Clear Problem Motivation**: The paper effectively motivates the problem by highlighting the importance of predicting cellular responses to novel drugs for drug discovery and personalized medicine. It also clearly articulates the limitations of existing methods, particularly their inability to generalize to new cell lines.

## Weaknesses
Despite its strengths, the paper has several weaknesses and areas for improvement:
- **Limited Reproducibility Details**: While the dataset and data splitting are described, the paper lacks detailed information on preprocessing steps, such as how control gene expression vectors are calculated and how differentially expressed genes (DEGs) are identified. Additionally, the code and pre-trained models are not publicly available, which hinders reproducibility.
- **Insufficient Ablation Studies**: The ablation studies are limited to a comparison with a naive fine-tuning approach. More comprehensive ablation studies, such as evaluating the impact of the drug-conditional adapter module and different bottleneck dimensions, would provide deeper insights into the method's design choices.
- **Need for Additional Metrics**: The paper primarily uses the R-squared (R²) metric for evaluation. Including additional metrics like mean squared error (MSE) or mean absolute error (MAE) would provide a more complete picture of the model's performance.
- **Complexity in Writing**: Some sections, particularly the introduction and related work, contain long and complex sentences that could be simplified for better readability. A clearer distinction between the contributions of this work and previous studies would also enhance the paper's impact.
- **Computational Efficiency Not Fully Explored**: The paper mentions that scDCA is more memory-efficient and faster than naive fine-tuning, but it does not provide detailed information on training and inference times. Including such data would strengthen the practical relevance of the method.

## Questions
- What specific criteria were used to select the top 20 differentially expressed genes (DEGs) for evaluation, and how do the results vary when using different thresholds for DEG selection?
- How does the performance of scDCA compare to other parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods, such as prefix tuning or LoRA, in the context of molecular perturbation prediction?
- Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the drug-conditional adapter's architecture, particularly the role of the bias vector $b_l$ in Equation 4, to clarify how molecular information is integrated into the model?
- What are the specific hyperparameters used for training scDCA, and how were they optimized? Including this information in the main text would improve reproducibility.
- How does the model handle the domain shift between pre-training on single-cell omics data and fine-tuning on molecular perturbation data? Are there any specific strategies to mitigate this shift?
