## Summary
The paper introduces a method called scDCA, which is a parameter-efficient fine-tuning approach for adapting single-cell foundation models (FMs) to predict transcriptional responses to novel molecular perturbations. The method uses a drug-conditional adapter layer to inject molecular information into the FM without modifying the original model weights, thereby preserving the rich biological representations learned during pretraining. The authors evaluate scDCA on four generalization tasks: unseen drugs, unseen drug-cell-line combinations, unseen cell lines in few-shot and zero-shot settings. They compare scDCA to several existing baselines and demonstrate that it achieves state-of-the-art performance, particularly in the more challenging zero-shot and few-shot generalization to new cell lines.

## Strengths
1. **Novelty and relevance**: The paper addresses a critical and underexplored problem in computational biology—predicting the effects of molecular perturbations in a zero-shot setting. This is highly relevant for drug discovery and personalized medicine.
2. **Efficient fine-tuning**: The proposed scDCA method is parameter-efficient, training less than 1% of the original model's parameters, which is a significant advantage in data-scarce settings.
3. **Robust evaluation framework**: The authors define and evaluate four distinct generalization tasks, including the challenging zero-shot cell line prediction, which is not commonly addressed in the literature.
4. **Clear methodology**: The paper provides a detailed description of the adapter architecture and how it integrates molecular information into the pre-trained scGPT model. The use of ChemBERTa for molecular embeddings is well-justified and aligns with prior work.
5. **Comparative results**: The results are presented in a clear and structured manner, with comparisons to multiple baselines and statistical measures (e.g., R² and standard error) to assess performance.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of implementation details**: While the paper describes the overall architecture and training strategy, it does not provide sufficient detail on the hyperparameters used for the adapter layers (e.g., the size of the bottleneck, learning rates, optimizer settings, and training duration for the adapter). This omission makes it difficult to reproduce the results or understand the full extent of the model's efficiency.
2. **Ambiguity in the input formulation**: The paper states that the input to scGPT during fine-tuning is the control gene expression vector of the cell line, but the exact preprocessing steps (e.g., normalization, tokenization, and how the control is selected) are not clearly explained. For example, it is unclear how the log1p-normalized expression values are tokenized and how the molecular embedding is integrated into the adapter layers.
3. **Insufficient description of the adapter module**: The adapter module is described as consisting of a molecular projection layer, down-projection, residual block, and up-projection. However, the exact architecture of the two-layer neural network used for the molecular projection is not specified (e.g., activation functions, hidden layer sizes, and whether it is shared across all adapter layers or unique to each).
4. **Limited discussion of the pre-training data**: The paper mentions that scGPT is pre-trained on over 33 million cells, but it does not provide a detailed description of the pre-training data sources, how they were processed, or how the pre-training task (masked gene prediction) contributes to the model's ability to generalize to molecular perturbations. This is important for understanding the model's inductive bias and generalization capabilities.
5. **Evaluation limitations**: The paper acknowledges that the evaluation is constrained by the limited size of publicly available data, but it does not provide a thorough discussion of the data splits or how the choice of the top 20 differentially expressed genes (DEGs) affects the evaluation. It is unclear whether this selection is consistent across all tasks and how it was determined.
6. **Reproducibility concerns**: The paper does not provide a complete list of hyperparameters used for training the scDCA model, nor does it specify the exact versions of the pre-trained models (e.g., scGPT and ChemBERTa) used in the experiments. This lack of detail hinders reproducibility and makes it difficult to assess the robustness of the results.
7. **Ambiguity in the loss function**: The paper mentions that the loss function during fine-tuning is similar to the pre-training loss, but it does not explicitly define the loss function used for scDCA. This is a critical detail for understanding how the model is trained and optimized.
8. **Missing ablation studies**: The paper does not include ablation studies to evaluate the contribution of individual components of the scDCA architecture (e.g., the molecular projection layer, the residual block, or the use of ChemBERTa embeddings). Without such studies, it is difficult to assess the importance of each design choice.
9. **Limited discussion of the zero-shot setting**: The paper briefly mentions that scDCA can generalize to unseen cell lines in a zero-shot setting, but it does not provide a detailed explanation of how the model leverages the pre-trained weights to achieve this. A more in-depth analysis of the zero-shot generalization mechanism would strengthen the paper.

## Questions
1. **What are the exact hyperparameters used for the adapter layers (e.g., bottleneck size, learning rate, optimizer, number of training epochs)?** This is essential for understanding the efficiency and performance of the model.
2. **How is the log1p-normalized gene expression tokenized in the scGPT model during fine-tuning?** The paper mentions a feed-forward network for tokenization but does not specify its architecture or how it is applied.
3. **What is the exact architecture of the two-layer neural network used in the molecular projection layer of the adapter?** For example, what are the hidden layer sizes, activation functions, and whether the network is shared across all adapter layers.
4. **What specific versions of scGPT and ChemBERTa were used in the experiments?** This is important for reproducibility, as different versions of pre-trained models can yield different results.
5. **How were the top 20 DEGs selected for evaluation, and is this selection consistent across all tasks?** The paper does not clarify the criteria for selecting DEGs or how this selection impacts the evaluation.
6. **What is the exact loss function used during the fine-tuning of scDCA?** The paper states it is similar to the pre-training loss but does not provide a formal definition.
7. **Can the authors provide ablation studies that evaluate the contribution of each component of the scDCA architecture?** This would help clarify the importance of the drug-conditional adapter and the integration of molecular embeddings.
8. **How does the model achieve zero-shot generalization to new cell lines, and what aspects of the pre-trained scGPT model are most critical for this?** A more detailed explanation of the generalization mechanism would strengthen the paper's contribution.
9. **What is the exact data splitting strategy for the zero-shot and few-shot cell line tasks?** The paper mentions that one cell line is reserved for testing in the zero-shot task, but it does not clarify how the training data is structured or how the control gene expression is used in this setting.

RATING: 7
