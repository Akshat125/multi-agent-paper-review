## Summary  
This paper introduces scDCA, a parameter-efficient fine-tuning method for single-cell foundation models (FMs) to predict transcriptional responses to novel molecular perturbations. The method employs a drug-conditional adapter that conditions the FM on molecular embeddings without modifying its pre-trained weights, enabling generalization to unseen drugs and cell lines in both few-shot and zero-shot settings. The authors evaluate scDCA on the sciplex3 dataset, comparing it to existing baselines and demonstrating state-of-the-art performance, particularly in zero-shot generalization to new cell lines. The work addresses the challenges of data scarcity and domain shift in molecular perturbation prediction, offering a practical solution for drug discovery and cellular modeling.

## Strengths  
The paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of single-cell molecular perturbation prediction. The proposed scDCA method is well-motivated, addressing the critical need for efficient and generalizable models in the context of limited data. The use of a drug-conditional adapter is a novel and effective approach to integrating molecular information into pre-trained single-cell FMs while preserving their learned biological representations. This design choice is particularly impactful in zero-shot and few-shot settings, where the model must generalize to unseen cell lines with minimal or no training data. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering four distinct generalization tasks, and the results show significant improvements over existing methods, especially in the more challenging zero-shot cell line prediction. The paper also provides a clear analysis of the limitations of the approach, which demonstrates a thoughtful and balanced perspective.

The integration of ChemBERTa for molecular embeddings is a strong technical decision, as it allows the model to leverage pre-trained chemical knowledge without altering the input distribution of the FM. This is crucial for maintaining the integrity of the pre-trained gene-gene interaction patterns. The results section is well-structured, and the use of R-squared as the primary metric is appropriate for regression tasks. The authors also provide insights into the robustness of scDCA across different molecular targets, which supports the claim of broad applicability. Overall, the paper presents a novel and effective solution to a challenging problem, with strong empirical validation and a clear articulation of its contributions.

## Weaknesses  
While the paper presents a promising approach, there are several weaknesses and areas for improvement. First, the **ablation studies are insufficient** to fully validate the claims. The comparison to a naive fine-tuning approach is useful, but the paper lacks a detailed analysis of alternative adapter architectures, hyperparameter choices, and the impact of different molecular embedding models. This makes it difficult to assess whether the performance gains are due to the adapter design or simply the use of a pre-trained FM. Additionally, the **focus on top 20 differentially expressed genes (DEGs)** is not well-justified, and the paper does not provide gene-level performance metrics, which would help to better understand the model's behavior.

The **experimental setup could be more transparent** in terms of implementation details. The paper mentions hyperparameters such as the number of epochs and batch size but does not explain how these were selected or whether a hyperparameter search was performed. The **training procedure for the adapter layers** is also not fully described, including initialization strategies and whether the adapter is applied to all transformer layers or a subset. These omissions hinder reproducibility and make it challenging to assess the robustness of the results.

Another concern is the **lack of a comparison to other parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods** in the context of single-cell data. While the paper highlights the efficiency of scDCA (training less than 1% of the original model), it does not contextualize this in terms of how it compares to methods like LoRA or prefix-tuning, which are well-established in NLP and vision domains. This limits the ability to evaluate the novelty and efficiency of the proposed approach.

Finally, the **zero-shot results could be further validated** with a baseline that uses only control gene expression data (i.e., no drug information), which would establish a lower bound for performance. The paper also does not provide a **sensitivity analysis** of the model to the quality of the control data, which is a critical input for the method.

## Questions  
1. Could the authors provide a comparison of scDCA to other parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods (e.g., LoRA, prefix-tuning) in the context of single-cell data to better assess the novelty and efficiency of the proposed approach?  
2. How was the threshold of 20 differentially expressed genes (DEGs) selected for evaluation, and is there evidence that this number is optimal for capturing meaningful transcriptional changes?  
3. What is the impact of the control gene expression data on the model's generalization performance, and how robust is scDCA to noisy or incomplete control data?  
4. Could the authors provide a more detailed description of the adapter training procedure, including initialization strategies and whether the adapter is applied to all transformer layers or a subset?  
5. How does the performance of scDCA change when using alternative molecular embedding models (e.g., MolBERT, DeepDTA) instead of ChemBERTa, and is the method sensitive to the choice of molecular representation?
