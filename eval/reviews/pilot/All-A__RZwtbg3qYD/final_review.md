## Summary
The paper introduces HOPE, a novel parameterization for long-memory state space models (SSMs) based on the Hankel operator theory. It addresses the challenges of initialization and training instability in SSMs by proposing a method that leverages Markov parameters within Hankel operators. The authors provide theoretical analysis showing that HOPE-SSM is more robust and has non-decaying memory, and they validate these claims with empirical results on the sCIFAR-10 and Long-Range Arena (LRA) tasks. The method is claimed to require fewer parameters and to be more stable during training compared to existing SSMs like S4 and S4D.

## Strengths
- **Theoretical Foundation**: The paper is grounded in a strong theoretical framework, using Hankel operator theory and reduced-order modeling to explain the limitations of existing SSM parameterizations. The theorems (Theorems 1, 2, and 3) are well-justified and provide a solid foundation for the proposed method.
- **Empirical Validation**: The experiments on the sCIFAR-10 and LRA tasks are well-designed and relevant. The results show that HOPE-SSM outperforms or is competitive with state-of-the-art models like S4D and S5, particularly in terms of long-term memory and parameter efficiency.
- **Practical Benefits**: The proposed method is claimed to be more robust and easier to train, reducing the need for careful initialization and special learning rate schedules. This is a significant practical contribution, as it simplifies the training process and makes the model more accessible to practitioners.
- **Parameter Efficiency**: The paper highlights that HOPE-SSM requires only 1/3 the number of parameters compared to S4D for the same LTI system, which is a substantial efficiency gain in the context of large-scale models.

## Weaknesses
- **Clarity and Explanation**: The paper assumes familiarity with concepts like the Hankel operator, Markov parameters, and the bilinear transform, which are not clearly explained for a general machine learning audience. The distinction between "asymptotic stability" and "numerical stability" is mentioned but not fully elaborated.
- **Missing Implementation Details**: The paper mentions that HOPE-SSM is implemented by nonuniformly sampling the transfer function but does not provide a detailed explanation of how this is done in practice. The pseudocode in Algorithm 1 is helpful, but the paper should explain the steps in plain language to ensure accessibility.
- **Incomplete Ablation Studies**: The ablation studies are insufficient to fully understand the impact of the proposed changes. A more detailed analysis of initialization, training stability, and parameter count would help to better understand the contributions of the method.
- **Statistical Significance**: The paper reports test accuracies with medians and standard deviations for the LRA benchmark, but it does not provide a detailed statistical analysis to confirm the significance of the performance improvements. Including p-values or confidence intervals would strengthen the claims.
- **Comparison with State-of-the-Art Models**: The paper does not compare HOPE-SSM with the Mamba model, which is noted as a state-of-the-art SSM in the introduction. Including this comparison would better contextualize the performance of HOPE-SSM.
- **Discussion of Limitations**: The paper should include a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed method, such as the behavior when $t > n$ and the computational trade-offs. This would help to provide a more balanced view of the method's applicability.

## Questions
1. What is the exact relationship between the Markov parameters and the system matrices in the HOPE-SSM? Is the system reconstructed from the Hankel matrix during training, or is it implicitly defined?
2. How does the bilinear transform affect the memory behavior of the HOPE-SSM? The paper claims that the continuous-time system associated with the Hankel matrix has non-decaying memory for $t \in [0, n]$, but the bilinear transform is used to discretize the system. A more detailed explanation of this interaction would be helpful.
3. What is the impact of the sampling period $\Delta t$ on the model's performance? The paper uses $\Delta t = 0.1$ in the noise-padded sCIFAR-10 experiment. It would be useful to see how the performance of HOPE-SSM varies with different values of $\Delta t$ and whether the model is robust to this hyperparameter.
4. Are the results in the LRA benchmark statistically significant? The paper reports median and standard deviation over 5 random seeds, but it is not clear whether the differences between HOPE-SSM and other models are statistically significant. A more detailed statistical analysis (e.g., p-values or confidence intervals) would strengthen the claims.
5. What is the computational overhead of the nonuniform sampling in HOPE-SSM? The authors claim that the computational complexity is the same as S4D, but the nonuniform sampling of the transfer function may introduce practical overhead. A runtime comparison or memory usage analysis would be helpful to confirm this.
6. How does HOPE-SSM perform on tasks with variable-length sequences? The paper mentions that HOPE-SSM is capable of handling sequences with varying lengths, but the experiments are conducted on fixed-length datasets. A more thorough evaluation on variable-length tasks would be a good next step.
