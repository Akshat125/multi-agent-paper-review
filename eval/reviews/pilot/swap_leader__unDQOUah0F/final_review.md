## Summary  
The paper introduces VideoWebArena (VideoWA), a benchmark designed to evaluate the agentic capabilities of long-context multimodal models in video understanding tasks. It categorizes tasks into two main types: skill retention (learning from video demonstrations to perform tasks efficiently) and factual retention (extracting specific, often incidental, information from videos to complete tasks). The benchmark includes 2,021 tasks across six domains (Reddit, Classifieds, Shopping, Shopping Admin, GitLab, and Map) and 74 manually crafted videos totaling nearly four hours. The authors test three baseline agents (video summary, video frame, and video agents) using models like GPT-4o and Gemini 1.5 Pro, finding significant performance gaps compared to human benchmarks. The results highlight the limitations of current models in agentic reasoning and video understanding, emphasizing the need for future improvements in this area.

## Strengths  
The paper makes a compelling case for the need of a benchmark focused on long-context video understanding for agentic models, a gap that is well-documented in the background section. The design of VideoWebArena is thoughtful, with a clear taxonomy of tasks that distinguishes between skill and factual retention, each with well-defined objectives and evaluation criteria. The inclusion of intermediate intents for factual retention tasks is a strong methodological choice, as it decouples video understanding from agentic planning, enabling more precise analysis of failure modes. The benchmark is also notable for its real-world relevance, as it mirrors practical scenarios like web navigation, shopping, and information retrieval. The reproducibility of the environment is enhanced by leveraging existing frameworks (WebArena and VisualWebArena) and providing access to the videos and tasks online. The experimental results are comprehensive, showing consistent performance gaps between models and humans, and the paper's open-source nature encourages further research and development.

## Weaknesses  
While the paper presents a novel benchmark, the methodology for task creation and evaluation lacks sufficient detail to ensure full reproducibility. For instance, the process of cross-validation for task quality is described only briefly, without clear criteria for what constitutes a valid or understandable task. The paper also does not elaborate on how the 74 videos were selected or curated, nor does it provide a detailed breakdown of the task creation workflow. Additionally, the baseline agents, while varied, are not thoroughly explained in terms of their implementation specifics (e.g., exact prompts used for summarization or frame sampling strategies). The results section could be strengthened by including more analysis of the performance trends, such as why the skill retention tasks show a performance drop when tutorials are added, and how the intermediate intent success rates correlate with final task success. The paper would also benefit from a more explicit discussion of the limitations of the current benchmark and how future work might address them.

## Questions  
1. Could the authors provide a more detailed description of the cross-validation process for task creation, including the specific criteria used to assess task understandability and completion?  
2. How were the 74 videos selected and curated? Were there specific guidelines or constraints for the types of videos included in the benchmark?  
3. What are the exact prompts or instructions given to the GPT-4o and Gemini models when generating actions for the baseline agents?  
4. Why do the skill retention tasks show a performance decrease when tutorials are added, and what mechanisms might be causing this "negative noise"?  
5. How do the authors plan to address the limitations of the Set-of-Marks observation space, particularly in capturing visual context and layout information?  

RATING: 7
